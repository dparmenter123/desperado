Re: Solaris, finally an appropriate name.

Not being a reader of arcane science fiction, I wasn't able to find a
copy of Lem's novel.  But as an occasional student of literary
criticism, I did have a access to a copy of "Alien Encounters: anatomy
of Science Fiction" by Mark Rose (Cambridge MA: Harvard University
Press, 1981), which includes a reasonably extensive discussion of
Stanislaw Lem's novel "Solaris" in ch.3, pp. 82-95.

I quote from Rose's comments, which you might also find enlightening:

"Lem's initial move in Solaris is to choose as problematic a sign of
the nonhuman as possible. ...

"By making Solaris itself as unyieldingly problematic as possible, Lem
shifts the narrative emphasis ...

"Has the ocean [of Solaris] failed to respond to human overtures
because it is serenely contemptuous of mankind? ...

"as the novel proceeds, the inquiry turns more and more emphatically
into an analysis of the human motives behind the whole Solarist
enterprise ...

"And late in the novel we hear about the scholar Muntius, who regards
the Solarist enterprise as 'the space era's equivalent of religion:
faith disguised as science.' ...

"According to Muntius, 'Solaristics is a revival of long-vanished
myths, the expression of mystical nostalgias which men are unwilling
to confess openly.' ...

"Lem attempts to keep [Solaris] his sign of the nonhuman as empty, as
nonreferential as possible ...

"In Solaris, however, where there is little sense of joy, the complex
of protective barriers and enclosures seem rather to be an expression
of agoraphobia, the fear of open places ..."

Of course these comments refer solely to Lem's work of fiction, not,
it must be understood, to anything else that might by pure coincidence
have the same name.

Regards,  Rolf

A writer looks at the world.
>From Smiling Jack Sopka, nearly two years ago:

THE TEMPTATION TO EXIST, by E. M. Cioran; translated by Richard Howard.
Seaver Books. 222 pp $17.95.

THE TROUBLE WITH BEING BORN, by E. M. Cioran; translated by Richard Howard.
Seaver Books. 212 pp. $8.95 (paperback).

By Fanny Howe
Special to the Boston Globe

"All my books are more or less autobiographical - a rather abstract form of
autobiography, I admit," writes E. M. Cioran, a Rumanian philosopher who has
lived in Paris since 1937.  The philosophic essay, as a form of confession
certainly has its precedents, but it nonetheless remains an original way of
positioning oneself.  The writer's response to the world is what you get; and
no world.

Cioran is a committed pessimist.  The titles of some of his books include "A
Short History of Decay" and "Drawn and Quartered."  "The Temptation to Exist"
includes an introductory essay by Susan Sontag, and it gives her response to
his response in terms which often mirror his.

Cioran is Gnostic.  The Gnostic view, like his, sees birth as a calamity.
Captive in matter and in time, under the domination of a deceiving "father",
mankind reproduces its misery from one generation to the next.  Cioran starts
from this point: Humanity, fallen into time, invents reasons to exist.
Civilization is at its best for him when it is, in contemporary terms, at its
worst - that is, when it is elitist, intolerant and self_referential.  This
goes for the church too.

In "The Temptation to Exist" there are vituperative essays about Europe,
literature, saints, Jews, philosophers and an open letter to a writer that
assaults the act of writing.  Fiction is especially revolting to Cioran, who,
for all his spleen, has humor.  Given that his attack on writing comes from
the pen of a writer himself, this letter is about as self-critical as you can
get.  "Poem, novel, essay, play," he writes, "everything seems too long.  The
writer - it is his function - always says more than he has to say: he swells
his thought and swathes it with words. . . .Every word is a word de trop.  Yet
the question is: to write.  Let us write. . . . Let us dupe each other."

The word "dupe" is significant because it is possible to imagine him, under
other circumstances, as a comedian, given the way his language trembles at the
edge of caricature.  Even at his most poisonous and passionate, there is a
touch of the poseur.  This touch is in a sense the confirmation of his theory.
The world is artificial.  So if Cioran is serious, he has to be joking.

In the collection of aphorisms, published simultaneously by the same press and
translated as well by the poet Richard Howard, Cioran's thoughts are reduced
to their essence.  In terms of wit, wickedness and insight, you couldn't get a
more potent dose than this.

Some examples:

        "Having always lived in fear of being surprised by the worst, I
        have tried in every circumstance to get a head start, flinging
        myself into misfortune long before it occurred."

        "This is how we recognize the man who has tendencies toward an
        inner quest: he will set failure above any success, he will even
        seek it out, unconsciously, of course.  This is because failure,
        always essential, reveals us to ourselves, permits us to see
        ourselves as God sees us, whereas success distances us from what
        is most inward in ourselves and indeed in everything."

        "The certainty of being only an accident has accompanied me on all
        occasions, propitious or injurious, and if it has saved me from
        the temptation to believe myself necessary, it has not on the
        other hand entirely cured me of a certain vainglory inherent in
        the loss of illusions."

Statements like these, based in the vocabulary of those thinkers who prededed
phenomenology, are accessible to any reader.  Yet there is a quality to his
thought which is distinctly European and a little chilling in the eyes of an
American reader, especially in those essays regarding nations and Jews.  While
his comments on literature and art, on human behavior and our quest for
meaning in a time of fundamental despair are important, his pessimism gets too
close to the nihilism of our century.  He sometimes seems less a critic than a
spokesperson for that nihilism.  When his thought is that of the negative
mystic and he is tempted to construct a new form of salvation - or liberation
- for the person, you wish he wouldn't stop at temptation.

The life of some cheeses is brief but sunny: che`vres, farmer cheeses,
they taste of nothing but freshness and youth.  Other cheeses, like
Parmesan, start life young and simple but grow old and wise; some very
few, like Stilton, are to be eaten only after death.  The majority of
good cheese offer a choice: between those that live defyng death and
those that wait only to embrace it.  This latter is what imbues a
French Camembert with its special character -- that buttery perfume of
anticipated corruption.  We Americans could not love a God whose feet
smelled like ripe Camembert.  But we know they do not.  They smell, if
anything, like Cabot Vermont cheddar, aged fourteen months, cut straight
from the wheel.

        -John Thorne
