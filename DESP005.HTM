                     RAKING AND BURNING FOILAGE

||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||
As my old history professor used to say, "If people can't disagree
about something, they'll disagree about something else."  The issue,
perhaps somewhat artificial, is "Do you enjoy the foliage more or
less if you understand what's going on?"

I might add that while we have many nearly congruent explanations of
the mechanism of fall colors, no one has come up with the *function*
of fall colors.  Flowers are pretty because bees like pretty flowers
and plants need bees to survive.  Maybe trees need to have people
looking at them and saying "That sure is pretty!"

By the way, the NH and VT people who profit from the tourists driving
up to see the fall colors universally refer to the phenomenon as "the
foilage".

Subject: BIKE RIDING IN THE LEAVES

When the answers to the questions about the leaves all arrive, and
they all will be very technical I'm sure, I doubt that any will answer
the question in a fundamental way.  Oh, you'll get answers about
chlorophyll, and enzymes, and sugar, and starch, and light
reflectivity, and the absence of green dye, and frost, and decreased
daylight, and lessened moisture, etc., blah, blah.

Terrific.  Wonderful.  But why do leaves change color?

You see, that's like the question, "How do you walk?"  To that
question you'll get stuff about will and muscle response and synapses
and electrochemical-neural discharge and etc., blah, blah.  Oh, by the
way, if you use "will," you will have to explain what that means.

Wonderful stuff.  But how do you walk?  You are not aware of all those
complicated microscopic events that go on, yet you do it and you don't
know how.  You just walk, and very well too.  And you cannot explain,
nor can anyone, how you walk.  It's very simple. Fine. Tell me how you
walk.

Here's another one.  Tell me how to ride a bike.  I already know how.
How do I know how?  I can get on a bike and go from point A to point B
and not fall down.  That is the experiential way that I know how to
ride a bike.  I cannot tell anyone how, and I can ride a bike.  So, by
knowing how, I mean I can do it, not write it down.

Conceptualized experience determines the nature of the experience,
the experience reinforces the concept, the concept then in turn
further determines the experience, etc, etc, until the experience does
not contain any reality at all; it's just another concept.  Most
people do not make the distinction between a concept of something and
the actual experience of it.  That is really too bad, because what
happens is that they do not really EXPERIENCE anything. Conceptualized
experience--BAH!

Do it.  Experience it.  Smell leaves, kick them, roll in them, throw
them around, listen to them, love their colors.

Walk on streets, in the woods, in water.  Feel it.  Notice the way
your legs move and how the muscles pull, notice the way the bones in
your feet spread and the muscles pull them together.  Actually
experience walking...enjoy it.

Ride a bike.  Notice your cold nose.  Hear the rushing wind.  Notice
your heavy breathing and the tension in your arms and legs.

Figure out machines; EXPERIENCE LIFE.

suspected that science teachers just stand up there and wing it, telling

eight-grade science teacher said that the stimulus for color changes

to contradict much of what people are saying about cold weather, etc.

I can certainly believe what someone said about chlorophyll masking the
bright colors. It absorbs enormous amounts of energy in the red-yellow
part of the spectrum (so much that it fluoresces in the infrared region).
Chlorophyll is, after all, designed to do so, since it's part of feeding
Mr. Tree. The various colors arise for several chemical reasons, including
the presence of sugars, the partial conversion of chlorophyll to other
pigments, and the loss of chlorophyll from the leaf.

resembles the crinkly autumn-woods kind and a beaker of green methyl
alcohol, that is, if you don't set your kitchen on fire first.

This is not to suggest that you shouldn't, instead, do as My Man George

From: METOO::WINALSKI
To:   ALIEN::PARMENTER
Subject: Autumn leaves and indian corn

Most of the pigments in plants are caused by porphyrins, not sugars.
Chlorophyll is one example.  In a living leaf, it is the dominant pigment.
Hence, most leaves appear to be a shade of green.  In autumn, leaves stop
producing chlorophyll and the other pigments.  Finally, the leaves die
altogher and fall off the plant.  Leaves cease production of different pigments
at different times.  Chlorophyll is one of the first to go, which is why we
get to see some of the other colors, mainly yellows and reds.  When the leaf
dies, the leaf tissues oxidize and turn brown.  This conceals the remaining
pigments.  Multi-color leaves happen when one area of the leaf gets ahead of
other areas in the dying process.

Indian corn, or wild maize, has different colored kernels because the various
kernels have different genotypes.  Standard (I hesitate to say garden-variety)
corn is a carefully bred hybrid.  All of the kernels were fertilized by pollen
from the same variety of corn, and therefore they all look the same.  Breeding
of wild maize is unchaperoned, and so you can get all kinds of genetic makeup.
The situation is similar to breeding cats.  Kittens in a litter from a
pedigreed persian all look alike.  An alley cat's kittens usually are a mixed
lot.

--PSW
