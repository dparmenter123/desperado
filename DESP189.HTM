    Mike Gunderloy
    6 Arizona Avenue

    is publisher of a wonderful fanzine of fanzines called "Factsheet
    Five", published five times a year.  You'll find it at places like

    subscribe at $2 per issue for up to six issues.

    This thing is wigged out, a window into a hundred, a thousand, strange
    worlds.  Mike audits and reviews somewhere between a myriad and a
    plethora of privately published magazines, newspapers, cassette
    recordings and genuine what-not.  Here are just a few samples that
    caught my eye.

    - SPYBASE ($35 from Micro Associates, PO Box 5369, Arlington, VA 22205):
    This is what software should be: quick, easy to use, bulletproof,
    unique and useful.  SPYBASE is a specialized database, with something
    over 75,000 citations to references on 38,000 or so people and
    individuals in "the intelligence community, the right wing, Latin
    America, the East Coast foreign policy establishment, domestic
    surveillance, assassination theory, and big business."  Author Danial
    Brand has painstakingly indexed hundreds of books and periodicals, from
    COUNTERSPY to the WASHINGTON POST to books by Agee, Hougan, and

    guarantee they meet their own state's possession requirements,
    naturally.

    - A.C. Samish sent along another three sheets of bizarre art, including
    strange machines, physics, excited engineers, and half-naked women,
    along with bits of collage and cryptic messages of great urgency.  You
    can get your own copy by sending a 25-cent stamp to him at 2118
    Guadalupe #191, Austin, TX 78705.

    - The World Series of Poker is hosted every year by Binion's Horseshoe
    Casino & Hotel (128 Fremont, Casino Center, Las Vegas, NV 89101).  You
    can get on their mailing list and receive glossy publicity brochures
    about this wonderful event, just for the asking.

    - ZIGGURAT (PO Box 25193, Rochester, NY 14625) has issued a new edition
    of Mickey Spillane's I, THE JURY, "Abridged for the Modern Reader": all
    the way down to 4 pages, leaving in only the most essential sex and
    violence.  A copy can be yours for $1.

    - AMERICAN WINDOW CLEANER #14-15 ($6 from 27 Oak Creek Rd., El
    Sobrante, CA 94803):  A trade publication which is fun to read --
    you'll be amazed at how sophisticated the window-cleaning industry has
    become.  #14 reports on a new world record set at a recent convention,
    gives some hints on writing contracts, and of course discusses the
    latest in tools and techniques.  #15 loosens up a bit, with snappy
    comebacks to stupid questions and a feature on the windows of
    Disneyland as well as the usual serious stuff.

    - CHICKEN SLACKS #3 ($2.50 FROM Mary Fleener, 309 Oceanview Ave,
    Encinitas, CA 92924):  This is the Soul Issue of this comic of
    illustrated song lyrics:  Jim Siergey doing Screamin' Jay Hawkins, Ken

     Lots of love and strange characters here, getting down to the funkiest
     silent music possible.

    - JUST COWS ($14.95 from Video-SIG, 1030-C E. Duane Ave., Dept. FS-5,
    Sunnyvale, CA 94086):  The title of this video is perfectly
    informative; this one is indeed 17 minutes of just cows.  It's an

    requiring a lot of attention, and does that admirably well.  Cows

    kinds in this mag, and not a single one of them shows any signs of

I've been quiet about this for a long time, but I feel that it's time to
come out of the closet:

It was in Binghamton, back in '56 or '57.  Temple Israel's annual Purim
Festival was approaching, complete with costume competition.  My mother,
whose taste generally ran more to Doris Day, decided to dress me up like
The King.  She slicked down my hair, painted on long sideburns, squeezed
me into a pair of tight pants, sprang for a toy guitar (an extravagance
almost unheard of in those days), slapped a coupla coats of light blue
paint on a pair of old shoes, et voila -- I was the original Elvis
impersonator!

Jews have never been bothered by "the devil's music" -- we've always had
real problems to worry about, like being massacred -- so, after the gala
Parade of Costumes, I blew away the feeble competition, easily winning
first prize in the boys' division.  Afterwards, one of the local papers
had me pose for a picture with the girls' winner (Queen Esther -- big
deal).  "But Elvis didn't live in the time of Queen Esther," I pointed
out, making my lips go all pouty, just as He did.  The working press
told me to forget about that and play for the queen, which I did.  The
moment lives on in a faded clipping somewhere among my parents' papers.

I got out of the Elvis impersonation business before all the amateurs
crowded in, and I don't regret it.
