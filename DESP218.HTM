        An almost Norman Rockwell story.  On my morning walk I've been
        watching one house where about a year back they posted a big sign
        (evidently for their son) wishing him luck as he went into the army.
        Then, when he was sent to Saudi Arabia, the house blossomed with
        yellow ribbons.  A few weeks back there was a big banner welcoming
        him home,

        They've always had a flag on a permanent pole, but just recently the
        regular Stars & Stripes has been replaced with a Desert Storm
        souvenir edition American flag with a huge Desert Storm logo with
        flashing jets and flaming tanks, like you've seen on T-shirts, and a
        sky-blue background.  When the flag is flapping, it looks like it's
        ripped, or has had paint spilled on it, or something.

        I was always a bit turned off by those Civil War regimental flags
        that had the name of the regiment embroidered on the bottom stripe,
        but this flag is way more offensive than that.  I mean, I know
        they're patriotic and they want to show it, but what would one of
        George Bush's unconstitutional flag laws say about this flag?  And
        how come Bush never talks about those laws anymore?  Maybe Dan "Mr.
        Gravitas" Quayle could go to work on this neglected area of public
        policy.

        I was watching a documentary about the P51 Mustang, probably the
        greatest fighter plane of WWII (although the P38 Forked Lightning
        remains my personal favorite, not to mention the Hellcat).  This
        was a pretty conventional show using archival footage, canned rock
        music, and a radio announcer reading a script.  A typical
        Discovery Channel offering, in other words.  It started with the
        original development of the plane and then showed its use in
        various times and places in the war.  As the documentary arrived
        at the conquest of the Japanese home islands, the narrator talked
        about the role of the P51, including the casual acknowledgement
        that "all students of the era agreed" that the conquest was
        unecessarily brutal, that many civilians died unnecessarily,  and
        that the war was unquestionably won before any of this happened.
        The A-bombs weren't even mentioned. although obviously they made
        their contribution to the truth of the statement.

        This matter-of-fact remark would have gotten you tried for
        treason if you'd uttered it in 1945 and would have gotten you
        pilloried by politicians and the public press if you'd uttered it
        anytime between 1946 and say 1962, singled out as a softy and
        probably a Red, and yet, by 1991 it is acknowledged, accepted,
        well-known by "all students of the era".

        If we live in an information age, and we always say we do, maybe
        the experts will get their consensus together a little more rapidly
        on the small, medium, and large wars the US has participated in
        since then.

I was strolling the grounds of Spit Brook, Digital's most beautiful site,
taking in the trees and flowers, bugs and birds and big green frogs when a
gang of folks strolled by discussing virtual reality and I thought "I'll
bet a virtual disk is a lot more like a real disk than any virtual reality
could be like this reality."

Little gas bubbles come up from the bottom of the pond.  The lily pads
have already grown to two inches wide.  Grackles are going after tadpoles.
Dragonflies are flashing cobalt blue.  And that's just a little bit of
what I can see.  Termites eat wood, but they can't digest it.  They have
specially adapted organisms who live in their stomachs and digest the wood
for them. There's one kind of creature that inhabits human eyelashes and
another that inhabits human eyebrows.   Reality is very active and full of
detail.  The granularity goes way down.  And way up too.  Galaxies spin
away.

Maybe "virtual" means "rude approximation for limited purposes", but that
doesn't change the meaning of "reality".

Don't get me wrong, I'd love to don the helmet and glove, but would it be
any "realer" than Dickens, or some Stone Age storyteller?

I don't know if I'd care to be a field-test site for whatever I'd have to
don to have a computer-induced sexual experience.  It would probably have
moving parts.  The interfacing and useability problems seem daunting.

While we're on the subject, the fans at Fenway have apparently taken to
bringing blow-up love dolls to ball games for those boring moments between
big innings.  I guess they got tired of the beach balls.  The ushers stab
the beach balls whenever they got near one.  Do they also stab the love
dolls?

  Upon preparing for a recent trip into Quebec, I came
  across a French Phrase Book that no self  respecting
  English   speaking  tourist  of  a  French  speaking
  country should be without. Upon reading some of  the
  offerings,  I  immediately  thought  this would be a
  Desperado crowd pleasure (funny how the mind works).
  At  the very least I thought it would invoke tirades
  from  my  French   friends   about   some   of   the
  translations  and,  in turn, be worth a laugh; I can
  imagine a smart aleck "ugly American" using some  of
  these phrases.

  In  this  light,  I  submit  the  following  phrases
  extracted  from  "Wicked  French"  by  Howard  Tomb,
  published by Workman Publishing of New York, 1989:

  In Customs:
  -----------

  Bien sur, soyez le bienvenu pour reduire  ma  valise
  en miettes.
    (Of  course,  you're  welcome to tear my suitcase
     apart).

  Heureusement ce ne sont que des  valises  moches  de
  Louis Vuitton!
    (Lucky they're only tacky Louis Vuitton bags!)

  For Tiny Hotel Rooms:
  ---------------------

  Le lit est assez grande pour ma jambe.
   (The bed is big enough for my leg.)

  C'est une chambre pour un nain.
   (This is a room for a dwarf.)

  Toutes les chambres sont comme ca?
   (Every room is like this?)

  Y a-t-il tant de nains en France?
   (Are there so many dwarfs in France?)

  For The Clothing Store:
  -----------------------

  Cela aurait l'air extraordinaire.
   (This would look great.)

  Sur un extra-terrestre.
   (On an alien.)

  At the Louvre:
  --------------

   Facing the Mona Lisa:
   ----------------------

  C'est ca? Seulment suspendre au mur?
   (That's it? Just hanging on the wall like that?)

   Intelligent Comments About Art You Can Make Yourself:
   -----------------------------------------------------

  Les petits citrons  vert  de  Cezanne  vous  sautent
  pratiquement dans la bouche!
   (Cezanne's little limes almost leap into your mouth!)

  At the Restaurant:
  ------------------

  Le boudin noir est un peu trop lourd a mon gout.
   (Pig's blood sausage is just too rich for me.)

  J'ai mange du pate de merle puor aux dejeuner.
   (I had blackbird pie for lunch.)

  Je  reserve  la  lamproie  a  la bordelaise puor une
  occasion speciale.
   (I'm savin stew of blood sucking eels  for  a  very
   special occasion.)

  Sophisticated Wine Talk:
  ------------------------

  Ce Beaucastel rouge est calin et bien charnu.
   (This Beaucastel rouge is plump and amusing.)

  Quel superbe bouquet de noisettes et de sardines.
   (What a delightful aroma of hazelnuts and sardines.)

  Le Vouvray a des asperites mais il est sincere.
   (The Vouvray is pimply but forthright.)

  Ce vin a de la jambe.
   (The wine has great legs.)

  Mais il a la fesse fripee.
   (But its buttocks are wrinkled.)

  Le Chateau Montrose est une roulure sans gratitude.
   (The Chateau Montrose is an ungrateful bitch.)

  ------------------------------------------------

  On se tire, poupee.
   (Lets split, babe.)

I have three regrets in my life.  Here's one of them.  I was riding a
subway to Cambridge one day when the car was invaded by nine people all
dressed in bright red and carrying various household implements -- brooms,
telephones, spoons -- all painted bright red.  Their faces were made up
red.  It was part of a festival of performance art commissioned by the
trolley authority to celebrate the opening of some new stations.   They
stood there, striking various poses, but saying nothing.  It occurred to
me that, just as I was getting off the train, I could turn and speak out
in tones of Stentor:  "The problem with performance art is you can't tell
whether any particular thing is part of the art or not.  Is this speech
part of the performance art?"  Then I would turn and leave the train.  But
I didn't.  Hence the regret.

I must have said this somewhere before, but here I am saying it again:

     Performance art is to art as performance art is to performance.
