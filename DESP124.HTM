              THE TRAVELING PRESIDENT PROBLEM

                       Tod Malmedy

We present a new variation of the traveling salesman problem.
There are two differences.  First, the links are free but the
nodes, representing memorials, have either small positive or
large negative weights, and the goal is to maximize this
weight.  Second, unlike most problems, in which a solver is
allowed to exhaustively search the space of combinations for an
optimal solution, in this variation the value of the final
solution is penalized by the number of combinations tested
before finding it.  Under these conditions the L/D (Leave it
to Deaver) strategy can be proven to be the worst possible.

As long as we're talking about Gilbert & Sullivan, which is being
broadcast on the Primarily British System, I want to ask if
people are watching the new set of episodes on the PBS show Mystery! It's
a dramatization of the Wilkie Collins melodrama The Woman in White. This is
a very famous novel, with three supremely interesting characters in it, two of
which appeared in the first episode: the heroine, Marian Halcombe, a strong
and intelligent woman who is, in the novel, described as very ugly, being
in particular disfigured by her eyebrows. The irony is that the actress playing
her is Diana Quick, who played Julia Flyte in Brideshead Revisited, where
she was considered a raving beauty. Autre temps, autre moeurs, I guess. Then
there's the supreme villain Count Fosco, who hasn't appeared yet, and Mr.
Frederick (not a hairdresser) who is Marian's cousin and
a neurasthenic. This is a Victorian malady that none of us have time to suffer
from anymore. It's what happens when your sensibilities are so finely tuned
that normal stimuli are torture to you. Roderick Usher suffered from it in
the Poe short story. Mr. Frederick sits all day in a darkened room and entreats
his visitors not to slam the curtains. My husband says it comes from living a
sensorily deprived life, exacerbated by poor nutrition, and no sunshine or
fresh air or exercise. Lots of Victorian maladies could be ascribed to these
causes, like the vapors and green sickness, a disease of young ladies
who turned green from anemia.

        I happen to be in complete agreement with the professor cited
in this AP story:

       Professor Takes Comic Books Seriously: Donald Duck's On The
                                Curriculum

                           By ELIZABETH P. LATT
                         Associated Press Writer

   NASHVILLE, Tenn. (AP) - Donald Duck isn't just Mickey Mouse
stuff to a professor here.
   Vanderbilt University professor Donald Ault speaks of the man
who created the squawking character for Walt Disney with the same
reverence other English professors reserve for Dickens and
Shakespeare.
   Donald may be a quarrelsome, foul-tempered duck in the movies,
says Ault, but his character in comic books is ``incredibly
articulate,'' often philosophizing and providing the voice of
reason.
   He believes the Donald Duck comics are literary classics in the
same league with works by British poet William Blake. So he teaches
courses about both.
   His course about Donald's antics - ``Popular Narrative: Comics,
Film and Television'' - was offered to 70 students last fall. Ault,
42, will teach it again next spring.
   Donald's comic book adventures are regular classroom materials.
Some are moralistic - the unhappiness that comes when Donald looks
for a way to get rich without working. In others, he is the
gullible hero who triumphs over April Fool's jokes, backyard pests,
spies and rigged quiz shows.
   ``Anybody can identify with him,'' Ault said. ``Sometimes he
really comes out the hero, when you least expect it.''
   Ault, whose interest in comic books stayed with him from
childhood, praiss Carl Barks, the man who drew Donald for Disney
studios. The genius of Barks, who retired in 1966, was that he
``made Donald seem so real in a context that was so impossible,''
Ault said.
   He said fans pay up to $45,000 for oil paintings by Barks of
Donald and other Disney characters.
   Among those who have credited Barks with inspiring them are
movie makers Steven Spielberg and George Lucas, who wrote the
preface to a volume of Barks reprints.
   ``Carl Barks' world view involves poking fun at the
materialistic tendencies that all people have and praising their
more sociable, brotherly aspects,'' Lucas wrote.
   Since 1981, Barks' works have been republished in several
volumes. Ault wrote essays and provided material for the Carl Barks
Library of Donald Duck. Ault said he has found that reading comic
books enables students to ``activate those dormant parts of their
imagination'' and causes ``an interest in all sorts of cultural
phenomena.''
   ``If you just get into it ... and allow yourself to get into
tha world, it opens up all kinds of possibilities in how you
perceive things.''
