From: WILBUR::DIAZ

Now, I could be wrong about this, you understand, but I'm pretty sure that
the following prefixes are part of the "Systeme Internationale" (SI),
which is the international standard for metric and scientific prefixes
(I useta be a technical editor):

        mega (M): 10**6
        giga (G): 10**9
        tera (T): 10**12

Because this might cause confusion in Britain, one is supposed to avoid
use of "million" and "billion" and use either exponential notation or
such prefixes. I don't know of a standard prefix for 10**15 either.

Note that the really interesting thing is that, for example, a "megabit"
or "megabyte" is usually not 10 to anything, but is in fact a power of 2.
The VAX virtual address space consists of 4 "gigabytes": 2**30 ("giga")
bytes times 2**2 (that's 4, boys and girls), to take one example. So I
suppose a "terabit" is 2**40 bits, since a "K" is 2**10, an "M" is
2**20, and a "G" is 2**30.

Everyone else has already figured this out, I suppose, but I had a free
minute.... By the way, VAX BASIC sez that 2**40 is 1.09951e12--lots more
than 10**12.

From: SPIDER::COLLIER
To:   ALIEN::PARMENTER
Subject: One good comma deserves another.

I take substantial exception to J_ANGEL's fulminations about commas.
That they are misused is certainly true, but more people UNDERuse them
than OVERuse them.

The remarks about commas before 'because' seem off the wall.  In the
first place, clearly there are occasions when the necessity of a comma
preceding the word 'because' is beyond dispute, as in

        We learned only yesterday, because of non-delivery of mail,
        that your tove was slivey.

or
        The use of commas preceding words such as 'but', 'because',
        and 'and' is unexpectedly contoversial.

Presumably what was meant was something like this:

        When the word 'because' is used to establish a causal connection
        between an assertion immediately preceding it and the
        assertion following it (or a subject following it, preceded
        by 'of'), it should not be preceded by a comma.

But this, too, (even apart from being clumsily expressed) I would dispute.
I have consulted no authorities, but my gut tells me that as subordinate
avjectival phrases should be set off from their betters by commas, so, too,
should the subordinality of a 'because' phrase be flagged with that (sometimes)
lovely preceding comma.  Thus, note the NON-subordinate becauseness of:

        Q.  Why did Billy not go to school yesterday?
        A.  He did not go to school because he overslept.

versus the very subordinate, needing that oh-so-informative comma, usage of:

        Q.  How did Billy do in school yesterday?
        A.  He did not go to school, because he overslept.

||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||
Speaking for myself, I insert commas by a number of arbitrary
instincts. I almost always put them before the "and" in a series and
after any kind of introductory clause, and between any two entities
less than a sentence when I feel the need of a breathing space.  I
mean breathing space literally.  I test for comma location by reading
out loud.

And I can't follow that last example.

But I don't know too much grammar.

From: KHOTEK::BINDER

Tom, I have to agree with your assessment of poetry, that the words must
carry maximum, not minimum freight.  In support of this argument, I offer a
short poem that I saw recently, one written by a young man whose lover was
murdered:

        My love, the world has stolen you away
        With evil, brutal pain.  I know that you
        Did not depart with freely given will.
        My life was touched by sweetly shared amour,
        And never shall you wander far away
        From thoughts and dreams of time we shared in joy.
        You touched my heart and left me richer than
        I should by rights have been.  I won't forget.

I have a copy of this poem because it so exquisitely expresses the feelings
associated with the discovery of such a tragedy.  I was very much moved by
it.  Anyway, note the subtle things, like the assurance (for himself, likely)
that her death was not suicide, and the use of "shall" in the second person
and "will" in the first person, both usages implying determination rather than
simple statement.  Poetry is NOT possible for computers to write; the best that
a machine can produce is a string of words that fit a metrical structure and
just MIGHT happen to make some sort of sense.
