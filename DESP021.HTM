              DAVE REACHES UP AND MUNGS THE I/O PAGE

||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||

From: CASTOR::D_DONCHIN
To:   PARMENTER
Subject: Put this in your LCP-5 box and smoke it

Monday afternoon, Reid Brown, DECwest product manager, made a presentation
on a new project called Seahorse.  Simply put, Dave Cutler and company are
going to come out with the first implementation of the micro-VAX architecture,
about 6-months before the 1-chip VAX itself comes out of Hudson.  The CPU and
memory controller occupy two quad-height boards that plug into a Q22 bus.
This will then be put in an LCP-05 (Micro/PDP-11) type box with integral
10MB Winchester, dual floppy, power supply, etc.  6 remaining Q22 slots
will be available for the disk controller, memory, extra peripherals, etc.
It will be compatible with all Q22 products.  (For those of you who don't
know: Q22 is the 22-bit addressable version of the LSI-11 bus.)

Some of the statements Reid made regarding Seahorse:

        "A VAX at LSI prices, ($1600 xfer cost for board set)"
        "Fast, reliable real-time machine (about 11/730 speed)"
        "Early market presence (Q3'84, which to Cutler, means January)"

The two-board implementation is being done with almost all off-the-shelf
parts.  One big part of the processor board, the "data path" chip, is being
done in custom VLSI by "Silicon Compilers Inc".  All design work, etc., is
being done at DECwest and in the vicinity.  (This use of "DECwest" bothers
me; it's as if it were a separate company.)  When the microVAX chip comes
out, a one-board version will be produced.

The intended applications are:

        Dedicated processor for OEM applications (a VAX in your refrigerator)
        A personal workstation

The idea with microVAX in general is to compete with chips like the MC68000
which will eat up a large part of a domain we ought to be a part of.  Seahorse
gets us there earlier.

Projected MLP prices for various systems are:

        $14,600 - 256KB, RD51, RX50; Seaboard application system
        $20,000 - 1MB, RD51, RX50, Ethernet; VMS Personal Computer
        $28,700 - As above but with Aztec 50MB disk

Seahorse has an obvious impact on two products: Low Cost Nebula and Orion
(J-11).  It may make LCN somewhat unattractive, but can coexist with J-11
since the J-11 is better at doing many small tasks - Seahorse is better
at fewer but larger tasks.

Reid then went on to give a short overview of Seaboard as it is today.  It
appears that Cutler has backed off from trying to make Seaboard into a
general purpose operating system and is again concentrating on making it
a very easy-to-use method of generating standalone real-time applications
for small VAXes.  Using "SeaPascal" (by the way, the "Sea" in all this
stands for Seattle), you develop your program on a regular VMS system,
compile it, and link it with the Seaboard executive and RTL routines,
then copy (somehow) the resulting image to your Seaboard system.  You can
run the program under VMS with the Seaboard system services emulated or
debug it on the Seaboard system itself with a full-featured debugger
"SeaView" (!).

While Reid was describing how parts of the Seaboard multi-tasking and
multi-processing worked, he kept saying things like "Dave reaches up and
mungs the I/O page" or "Dave remaps the memory page", which caused me to
wonder out loud how they had managed to clone thousands of little Cutlers
and stuff one into each Seaboard box....   Anyway, it appears that Dave
is happy doing microcode for Seahorse.  (I wonder if the diagnostics are
called "Cecil the Seasick Sea Serpent"?)

From: ORPHAN::BODGE

Subject: Bells and Whistles

Actually, the origin of this phrase greatly predates the advent of the
steam locomotive or, for that matter, the Industrial Revolution.  It
is a corruption of a lost Biblical phrase - lost because Gutenburg
inadvertantly dropped a page of type on the floor and couldn't be
bothered to reconstruct it.  Here's the story:

You may not know that there was a 13th apostle to Jesus.  There was;
he was St. Omar the Dyspeptic.  He doesn't appear in "The Last Supper"
because he was down the hall at the time.

In any case, after the Crucifixion, the apostles dispersed to spread
the word.  St. Omar chose to go to a backwards area (now Albania) to
preach to the Blottic people.  Now, the Blottics were a nomadic people
and also exceptionally stupid.  In fact, shortly after the arrival of
St. Omar, they packed up their tents for their annual move to higher
ground, but their leader was confused about East and West and as a
result the Blottics walked and rode into the Adriatic and were lost
to history.  St. Omar gave up the apostle business and became a
travelling representative for a Greek wine firm.

During his short preaching career, however, St. Omar displayed two
characteristics: his sermons were highlighted by a lot of handwaving,
props, and general flim-flammery; and he burped a great deal.  In fact,
his delivery was almost one continuous belch; and as a result, the
phrase "belched epistles" was applied to his efforts.  In a short time,
this phrase became associated with all the handwaving etc.  The phrase
was carried out of St. Omar's area shortly before the Blottic's last
migration, passed down for a few centuries, and eventually twisted
into the form that we have today.

The way I have been told, the original DEC-10 Son of Stopgap (SOS), was,
naturally, a later version of the editor called Stopgap.  Stopgap was a line
editor written for DEC-10's at Stanford some years ago, as a temporary hack
for people who tended to mangle files with good ol' TECO.  I don't know what
editor was envisioned as the "ultimate" editor for which Stopgap and SOS were
intermediates, or if it was ever built.

Stopgap was the predecessor to SOS.  It was an editor written as a stopgap
measure because the real editor wasn't ready in time to be released with
the operating system (I forget which one this happened to--TOPS-10, perhaps?).
When the real editor was finally released, they decided to call it "Son of
Stopgap," or "SOS" for short.

Well, RE: Son Of Stopgap, actually.

The story goes that in the old days of TOPS-10, all you could edit with
was TECO or something called LINED, which represented the opposite ends
of the editing spectrum. (LINED had about the power of your average
BASIC interactive editing -- if you don't like line #42, replace the
whole line).

Some American university (pretty precise, this, isn't it?) thought they'd
rather like something in-between, so they wrote their own editor and
called it 'Stopgap'. The next editor was, not unnaturally, called
'Son of Stopgap'.

                        MORE ORIGINS: CMS

From: METOO::WINALSKI
To:   ALIEN::PARMENTER
Subject: CMS

The CMS I was referring to that ran on the S/360 mod 40 was CAMBRIDGE Monitor
System, the freebie version later released with CP/67.  When the whole thing
was repackaged and sold as VM/370, the name was changed to CONVERSATIONAL
Monitor System.  "Cambridge Monitor System" was no longer appropriate because
they moved the development team from the Cambridge Scientific Center to
Endicott, NY.

CMS is, and always has been, much more than a virtual batch system.  The
"virtual system console" is a timesharing terminal, just like on a VAX or
PDP-11.  Most of the other IBM so-called timesharing systems are really
interactive editors with a remote batch submission facility attached.  You
can't write interactive programs on them.  You can on CMS.

The amount of applications software, IBM- and third-party-supplied, that runs
on CMS far exceeds anything available for any DEC operating system.  The
Pro-350 is a much better machine, but it will have a lot of catching up to do
in the software area to match CMS running on a 370-based IBM PC.

--PSW
