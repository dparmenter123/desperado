        Delightful reading of the month, "Southern Reader" (Hey!  That's
        me all over!), two big articles under the heading "Elvis:
        Suspicious Minds Want to Know!".

        First is "Camp Elvis: Totem and Taboo, Hawaiian Style", an attempt
        to rescue the King's movies from those cranks who find them
        shallow and cheap and not particularly representative of the
        volcanic force of the King by making the good old
        deconstructionist, post-modern argument that they are ironic:
        "Like tropical fruit punch, five percent juice and ninety-five
        percent water, 'Paradise Hawaiian Style' does not pretend for a
        mement to offer any real, fresh-squeezed paradise.  Instead, with
        a qualification that perfectly toes a fine line between parody and
        self-parody, stylized artifice and just plain bad imitation,
        'Paradise Hawaiian Style' relishes its own artificiality -- its
        'tackiness' in [Greil] Marcus's words -- without being cynical or
        smug."  Et cetera.

        Then there's Marcus himself, in "Elvis Without Music", in which
        the quotes are better than the article.

        Such as:  "There was always something supernatural about him.
        Elvis was a force of nature.  Other than that he was just a t2d.
        A big dumb hillbilly a couple points smarter than his mule who
        wandered out from behind his plow one day to cut a record for his
        sainted mother and never came back, which he probably woulda
        forgot to do evin if he hadnt've been whisked up.  Why shouldn't
        one physical corpus be capable of containing these two seeming
        polarities simultaneously?  Especially if it's from outer space,"
        opined Lester Bangs.

        And, "He wanted to be an example to young people.  People say
        because later on it was found that he had used drugs, that
        therefore he could not be a good example.  They opverlooked the
        fact that he never used illegal drugs.  It was always drugs
        prescribed by his physician," recorded Richard Nixon for his
        Presidential Library.  Perhaps this was a point Nixon understood
        well.

        And, "Thank you, without you I'm nothing, thank you, too, without
        you I'm nothing,  I love you, thank you, without you I'm nothing,
        I love, thank you, too, without you, I'm nothing, I love you, too,
        Elvis -- without Elvis you're nothing," as Madonna put it, closing
        out the "Blond Ambition" tour, kissing the cast and crew goodbye
        as they dropped through trapdoors one by one.

        And, in the end, "Three weeks of extensive investigation have
        uncovered a dramatic answer to the riddle, 'WHO MURDERED J.F.K?'
        There's a sensational link between the death of Elvis Presley and
        that of the president.  Elvis was murdered by assassins who wanted
        to create a crackpot network of people who believed that he was
        still alive.  Why?  Because then the people who think Elvis is
        still alive would make the Kennedy conspiracy buffs look like
        crackpots, too.  Any new evidence suggesting that Lee Harvey
        Oswald didn't act alone could be belittled with 'Oh yeah.  And
        Elvis is working at a Burger King in Fremont.  Right.'  And there
        are astonishing similarities between the death of Elvis and the
        death of the president.  Elvis died on the toilet.  Kennedy died
        in Dallas.  Kennedy had a secretary named 'Lincoln.'  Presley's
        manager was named 'Colonel.'  There were 'colonels' during the
        Civil War -- a war run by 'Lincoln.'  Kennedy died in 1963;
        Presely in 1977.  Both numbers have four digits.  Ed Sullivan shot
        Elvis from the wiast up.  Kennedy was shot from the head down," as
        some guy said on the radio once.

UPI -
HIGH COURT STRIKES DOWN NATURE'S FIRST LAW

        In a decision sure to cause major controversy for several minutes,
the US Supreme Court struck down the First Law of Nature, judging it to be
unlawfully discriminatory.

        Though never actually written down, the First Law of Nature is
generally accepted by the American Bar Association to be, "If an individual
or collection of individuals puts into practice or causes to be put into
practice an action which, measured against the judgement of a reasonable
person, is unwise or lacking in basic common sense, then the aforementioned
individual or collection of individuals shall suffer all consequences
resulting from that action, up to and including loss of life."

        Translation:  "You do something stupid, you die."

        In a ruling handed down yesterday, George Bush's Supreme Court
ruled that such a law was too vague and unfairly discriminatory.  Justice
Rhenquist, author of the decision, wrote, "The terms 'unwise' and 'basic
common sense' are never defined anywhere, and are left to the
interpretation of the presiding judge....[which could lead] to mutiple
standards of justice." The court also felt that the penalty was too vaguely
defined and too harsh.  Writes Rhenquist, "It is theoretically possible for
an individual to lose his/her life by, for example, sticking their tongue
across a 120 volt outlet.  While such an action would clearly demand
explanation from the individual attempting it, it is not Our [sic] opinion
that such an action warrants the penalty of death, particularly if the
outlet in question lacks a label warning of such practice..."

        In striking down the First Law, the Court also mandated that steps
be taken to correct the discrimination the law imposed.  Affirmative Action
has already drafted a set of standards.  Though not final, the suggestions
include:

        o Requiring employers to hire a set percentage quota of stupid
          people (most employers with union contracts already meet the
          proposed standard),

        o Requiring all non-profit organizations receiving public funds (PBS,
          National Endowment for the Arts, et al) to spend a set percentage
          of those funds promoting stupidity (the NEA, based on recent
          censorship mandates, already qualifies),

        o Equal time for the views of the stupid on national issues,

        o A constitutional amendment banning discrimination based on
          stupidity, and

        o A national TV and press campaign to raise the nation's awareness
          of the plight of stupid people (the draft suggests the slogan,
          "Stupid Is Beautifull [sic]").

        When interviewed, Dan Quayle said, "It's the most significant step
forward this country has taken since Neil Diamond set foot on the moon."

Re: the following item in Desperado #3055:

>       As if that weren't enough, the Russian Republic is proposing to

Apparently, this is a hoax perpetrated by a magazine called "Forbes FYI",
a very fluffy "lifestyle" quarterly supplement to Forbes magazine.  It must
be for those millionaire subscribers Forbes keeps bragging about, because
it sure ain't aimed at me.

Anyway, one morning in mid-November, I heard on NPR's Morning Edition as I
was waking up a story relating how the Soviet Foreign Ministry was very
angry about stories in the press indicating that Lenin's corpse was for
sale.  They stated that the story was completely false.  It seems that the
story was picked up by AP and run unchecked in a number of papers in the US
(not surprising, 'cause it's a good story).  The NPR story went on to
interview FYI's editor, Christopher Forbes, who thought the whole thing
very funny, and wondered about the absence of a sense of humor on the part
of the other editors who ran the story in their publications.  All in all,
a very funny tempest in a teapot.

For those who are interested, the story does appear in FYI, the November
25, 1991 issue, page 25.  Accompanying the story is a photo montage of
Lenin's corpse, in a plexiglass box, with a rose-in-vase and an ice bucket
complete with champagne on top, with two couples behind in formal dress
standing around trying to look like they're having a good time.

The last three paragraphs of the story are:

"A condition of the sale is that the Lenin corpse not be used for any
"commercial, or improper" purpose, the deed of purchase to be administered
by the International Court of Justice at The Hague, in the Netherlands,
making the conditions of sale enforceable by that international legal
community.

"Description:  Mr. Lenin's body was embalmed at his death in 1924, and
stored in a sealed, climate-controlled glass casket.  (Shades of Sleeping
Beauty!)  It has been periodically re-embalmed.  Every five to ten years,
the skin, somewhat yellowed but by no means jaundiced-looking, requires a
special application of preservative, or "waxing."  Under the terms of sale,
maintenance is to be provided only by qualified Russian mortuary
specialists from the Interior Ministry, expenses to be paid for by the
purchaser.  (Estimated annual upkeep:  $10,000-$15,000, varies with
climate.)

"Obviously, the Lenin corpse is not for everyone.  But as a conversation
piece, it would certainly have no equal.  You might have some explaining to
do to the lady of the home, but the item is fairly compact and could be
accommodated to fit in most large dens."

How long before magazines and newspapers start feeling the need to put
those silly smiley faces [;-)] on their satire?

local telephone companies to enter the information (including video)

even seen a telephone, and that he had never thought about newspapers.

in Pinpoint, Georgia, they had no telephone and did not use newspapers,
not even the editorial page, in their outdoor toilet.
