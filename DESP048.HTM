Computer-generated poetry is my one toy.  For several years, I have
maintained a BASIC program, copied from the Conway/Archer book
PROGRAMMING FOR POETS, the last 20 lines of which are phrases the
user plugs in.  One also supplies parameters for line length, stanza
length, degree of repetition of phrases, and a few other variables.

relatedness of the user input, but the results are occasionally weird

        Sweet clarity; sweet sad delusion;
        Jung at heart in the heart's hard darkness

        soaring

I was leafing through some back issues of  Desperado.   Leading  off  issue

individuals who don't know any better.  I became one of these when I  found
that  FORTRAN  and  BASIC  did not afford me sufficient scope for my typing
talents.  I've been thinking about the rest of my colleagues.  We don't get
out much, I grant you, but we do get around.

Real applications programmers know COBOL.  Only COBOL.

Real applications programmers can type 50 words a minute.  They have to.

Real applications programmers write memos with RUNOFF and EDT.

Real applications programmers take courses from Ed.  Services  in  Bedford,
but  they  don't  do  the labs.  If they *do* the labs, they take all their
programs home on a 600 ft.  magtape.

Real applications programmers have to go to meetings.

Real applications programmers go home at 5 o'clock.

Real applications  programmers  are  convinced  the  movie  TRON  is  about
applications programming.  Who else thinks of the User as a god?

Real applications programmers don't know anyone on third shift.

Real applications programmers  work  in  places  like  Merrimack  and  West
Concord.  No real applications programmer works in the Mill or Spit Brook.

Real applications programmers  work  for  Colonel  Crawford,  at  least  in

Real applications programmers see no difference  between  Coke  and  Pepsi.

Real applications programmers feel daring and guilty when they use GO TOs.

Subject: Hands across the water: one means take, two means hit away

All this talk about cricket, American football, and basketball reminds
me of a true story involving John McGraw, the great manager of the old
New York Giants baseball team, and King George V (grandson of Victoria,
grandfather of Elizabeth II, ruler of Great Britain from 1910-1935).

During a visit to the United States, King George attended a ballgame
at the old Polo Grounds as a guest of the Giants.  He sat in the stands
with McGraw, who explained the game's action to him.

At one point, a Giant player bunted a teammate from first to second.
McGraw said to the King, "Our man bunted, your Majesty -- he
sacrificed his turn at bat so that our other player could move into
scoring position."

The king considered this for a few moments and then replied, "Rather
sporting of the chap, what?"

In the third volume of the Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy trilogy,
"Life, the Universe, and Everything," cricket is revealed to be the
playing out of an unpleasant racial memory.  I recommend the book
heartily to everyone who is or isn't interested in cricket.

--J.

From:   DECO::MARKETEER
To:     @CLUSAN.DIS
Subj:   A Name for Clusters

Here is the criteria we in VAX Base Products Marketing developed for a
VAX "Cluster" name:

 1.  Include reference to VAX - to build on industry brand awareness.
 2.  Describe a single entity - any collection of VAX components which
     includes the CI.
 3.  Commonly used in singular form - the XXXXX is singular (even
     though consisting of multiple VAXes).  More than one XXXXX is
     able to take on the plural form.
 4.  Carries no VAX-like number - the XXXXX can consist of many VAX
     components and must not be associated uniquely with any one of
     them.
 5.  Conveys a product message - a new way to build systems; extending
     VAX in a new dimension to new applications - high availability,
     high performance.
 6.  Catchy, probably short, to-the-point, easy-to-say and write.
 7.  A name which will be remembered identified or associated with
     Digital, VAX.
 8.  Trademark-able.
 9.  Unique, conveys VAX, CI uniqueness
10.  In a form capable of accepting future VAX products -
            VENUS, SCORPIO, NAUTILUS, RAXX will be components of XXXXX
11.  Comparable to other current and planned extensions to VAX and
     Digital products - examples: VIA, microVAX, microPDP11
12.  Not just VAX, more than VAX.
13.  Long lasting -  it has to last 8-10 years

The current "straw horse":  ultraVAX

Other names considered and not selected:  multiVAX, VAXplex, VAX/XL,
OmniVAX, DECade, Uniplex, VAX PACE, HyperVAX, SuperVAX, ULTRAnet,
DECplex.

If you do not agree with the criteria, please change it.  If you have
a better straw horse, we will use it.

Thanks for your help and interest.

First potpourrus (a la hippopotamI):

The recent banter about whether IBM builds its PC or not prompted me
to kick in this somewhat related tidbit.

All you hackers out there who peruse the tail end of Byte magazine
for deals on chips and hardware you'll never really get around to
using have probably seen the ad for the "Big Board".  The "Big Board",
also known as the "Ferguson", is a single-board Z80 system chock-full
of RAM and all kindza peripheral chips.   It's a lot of stuff for the
money, but looks pretty much like one of the million other California-
garage-shop products on the market.

It's a little known fact that this board is basically the Xerox 820.
Xerox bought the design for the board from Ferguson, whoever he is,
laid it out differently, put it in a box, and called it the Xerox 820.

Second potpourrus:

Speaking of PC's, have you noticed something about the TV commercials
for DEC's and TI's PC's ?   They're interchangeable !!!

Third potpourrus:

"...getting it up on her Apple..." reminded me of this one.  During my
RCA days, I worked on the Aegis system, affectionately spoken of by the
Navy as "the shield of the fleet".  It's a cruiser-  or frigate-based
system designed to protect a fleet of ships.   We often spoke of RCA's
mission being to equip the Navy with

                 "Aegis Protective Shields".

Fourth potpourrus:

I heartily endorse Cibachrome.  The color quality is outstanding.  I
don't quite understand why it's so expensive, though.  I've had a few
Cibachrome prints made that were not quite a decimal order of magnitude
higher than the standard + to - to + process.  Being a positive-to-positive,
process, Cibachrome eliminates a step, and all my photography friends say
that the parameters of the process are very forgiving.  Maybe it's just
a low volume business.
