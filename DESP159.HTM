(from N.Y. Times 3/11/87 edition written by David E. Sanger)

                   Big Blue vs. the Bulgarian Megabite

   The U.S. & the Soviet bloc play a cat-&-mouse game with computer technology.
The Government tries to insure that nothing more powerful than an Apple II slips
through to Eastern Europe. Meanwhile, the other side is eager to demonstrate
that it already understands the technology, hoping to persuade the U.S. to relax
its export controls.
   According to a story making the rounds in Washington, American officials got
their hands on a Bulgarian-made 200-megabyte disk drive.
   "We didn't have the expertise to evaluate it, so we shipped the thing to a
large company we do a lot of business with," said one senior Government offic-
ial, warming to the story. The company, others volunteered, was I.B.M.
   For weeks, according to the tale, engineers tinkered and toyed, but even the
technological firepower of the world's largest computer company could not make
the Bulgarian disk drive go whir and click. Finally, the engineers did what any
frustrated customer would do: they called for service.
   "A few weeks later these two Bulgarians show up," eager to demonstrate the
capabilities of the machine, the Government official, who insisted on anonymity,
continued. "One of them is a woman, a rather large woman actually, who had very
few tools. But she started taking the drive apart, pulling out wires with her
bare hands."
   And as astonished technicians looked on, "she started stripping the wires
with her teeth," he said.
   By the end of the day the disk drive was up and humming. As the strong-
toothed Bulgarian packed up to go, the engineers presented her with a parting
gift: a pair of wire strippers.
   A spokesman for I.B.M. suggested yesterday that the episode may be apocry-
phal. But the Government official swore the story was true. By transferring
wire stripper technology, he said, the United States may have "put a lot of
Bulgarian dentists out of business."

EasyFlow:

  We don't claim Interactive EasyFlow is good for anything--if you think
  it is, great, but it's up to you to decide. If Interactive EasyFlow
  doesn't work: tough. If you lose a million because Interactive EasyFlow

    Fresh from Cornell, I remember the five-inch green magnetic strips
    that were the bane of many careless students.

    A friend of mine worked in one of the libraries, and as new books
    or magazines came in he would insert these adhesive strips into
    them.  Worked great.

    Now if I may wander from this discussion a bit...

    My friend and another guy were in a heated practical joke war.
    My friend, Steve, was a wiz at finesse.  The other fellow, Jack,
    was a wiz at...well...sledgehammer humor.  Overkill.  It was often
    just as hilarious, but a different style.  So one day Steve nails
    Jack nicely.  Jack retaliates with the old stand-by: a garbage pail of
    water propped against the door. (Consider it the anti-neutron bomb
    of the practical joke war -- it levels the house but leaves the
    people standing.)  So first thing in the morning, whoosh, Steve
    and I have very wet feet.  Yeah, we were roommates.

    So Steve, knowing Jack hates the smell of cigar smoke, buys a
    pack of the nastiest, cheapest ones he can find -- plus a short
    length of plastic tubing.  Jack has barricaded his room, you see.
    He expects sledgehammer humor in return, so he's thoroughly locked
    his room, and even stuffed a towel beneath the door to
    prevent...whatever.  Doesn't stop Steve.  Steve lights up his
    first White Owl (or Death Beak, I forget the brand), and puffs
    merrily through the plastic tube which pokes under Jack's door
    and past the towel.  Jack comes home later, steps inside, comes
    out thunderstruck.  When he figures out it was Steve he's delighted
    with Steve's ingenuity and goes off laughing to congratulate him.
    Steve -- just recovered from being nauseous in the bathroom from
    chain-smoking five Death Beaks -- graciously accepts the
    acknowledgment.

    But the fun's not over.

    Jack fires the next salvo.  Steve and I come home one day from classes,
    and everything in the room seems somehow...lighter.  Faded.
    Especially near the door.  Strange, yes.  I move the garbage can
    and notice it leaves a distinct SHADOW on the carpet.  Takes us
    two seconds to track down Jack and get a confession.  Yes, another
    old stand-by stunt: he took a hairdryer, held its nozzle under
    our door, fired it up, poured talcum powder into its intake and
    foooom.  Our room was deftly powdered.

    So...

    It's Steve's turn.  Steve works in a library, right?  Students study
    in libraries a lot, right?  In fact, pick any random student and
    say "he'll probably spend at least a minute in SOME library today"
    and you'll probably be right, right?  Steve works with, thus has
    unrestricted access to, thus could pilfer an armload of if
    the mood struck him, those little plastic security strips.

    Jack's an average student.  Goes to the library quite often.

    So one fine morning Steve slips into Jack's room when Jack is out
    and SATURATES Jack's belongings with pieces of little five-inch magnetic
    security strips.  Books, bookbag, jacket, pants, shoes, socks,
    newspaper, everything.  That day, I'm sure, Jack is such a magnetic
    anomoly that passing jet planes for miles around were zinging off course.

    Yup, Jack hits a library that day, his roommate with him.  They
    finish their business and his roommate steps through the security
    bar (a swinging bar that won't swing if you're trying to steal books),
    turns, and waits for Jack.  In a few moments, the roommate is on
    the ground in hysterics.  Jack had been nabbed and stopped.  Aghast,
    he starts pulling out books, all his own.  Still the bar won't swing.
    He sets down his bookbag entirely and tries to leave with empty hands.
    Whack, he hits the bar.  The security guy is leering at him now,
    The roommate has figured it out and is gasping on the
    ground outside.  So Jack empties his pockets.  Whack.  Jack takes off
    his jacket.  Whack.  He surely must have noticed the little
    green magnetic strips dropping from him like snowflakes, because
    his roommate reported later that Jack was cursing something like
    "I'll get you, Steve...no, I'll KILL you, Steve..."

    And that ended the war.  Finesse won out, and Jack rested his heavy
    head at Steve's feet.

    But to answer your question, no, I have no idea how they work.

Subject: Computer-lighting board nearly causes WWIII

    With the recent discussion of computer-controlled lighting
boards, I ask "How resistant to Electron Magnetic Interference
should these boards be?"  Not such as academic point as one would
think.  An audio engineer friend of mine related this incident:

    He was running sound for the Broadway production of "A Chorus
Line" a few years back.  Then-president Ford came to a performance.
Secret Service men everywhere.  One by the sound board, one by lighting,
etc.  All is quiet until about the mid-point of the play.  Then the
Secret Service man standing by the lighting board (an early Nicholson
model I believe) got a call on his walkie-talkie.  To reply, he depressed
the push-to-talk switch, sending out a couple of watts of RF, and
Presto! the entire theatre was plunged into inky darkness.  Chaos
ensues, PERT guns are drawn, etc., etc.  It completely wiped out the
CMOS memory of the lighting board.

    Questions: should mil-spec EMI resistance be built into only military
equipment?  Who would have thought that a lowly theater lighting board would
be of critical national importance if for only a few moments?  Could a
high-tech John Wilkes Booth use some knowledge such as this for the next
assassination attempt?

                A CODE OF ETHICAL BEHAVIOR FOR PATIENTS

 1.  DO NOT EXPECT YOUR DOCTOR TO SHARE YOUR DISCOMFORT.
        Involvement with the patient's suffering might cause him
        to lose valuable scientific objectivity.

 2.  BE CHEERFUL AT ALL TIMES.
        Your doctor leads a busy and trying life and requires all
        the gentleness and reassurance he can get.

 3.  TRY TO SUFFER FROM THE DISEASE FOR WHICH YOU ARE BEING TREATED.
        Remember that your doctor has a professional reputation to
        uphold.

 4.  DO NOT COMPLAIN IF THE TREATMENT FAILS TO BRING RELIEF.
        You must believe that your doctor has achieved a deep insight
        into the true nature of your illness, which transcends any
        mere permanent disability you may have experienced.

 5.  NEVER ASK YOUR DOCTOR TO EXPLAIN WHAT HE IS DOING OR
     WHY HE IS DOING IT.
        It is presumptuous to assume that such profound matters
        could be explained in terms that you would understand.

 6.  SUBMIT TO NOVEL EXPERIMENTAL TREATMENT READILY.
        Though the surgery may not benefit you directly, the resulting
        research paper will surely be of widespread interest.

 7.  PAY YOUR MEDICAL BILLS PROMPTLY AND WILLINGLY.
        You should consider it a privilege to contribute, however
        modestly, to the well-being of physicians and other humanitarians.

 8.  DO NOT SUFFER FROM AILMENTS THAT YOU CANNOT AFFORD.
        It is sheer arrogance to contract illnesses that are beyond
        your means.

 9.  NEVER REVEAL ANY OF THE SHORTCOMINGS THAT HAVE COME TO LIGHT
     IN THE COURSE OF TREATMENT BY YOUR DOCTOR.
        The patient-doctor relationship is a privileged one, and
        you have a sacred duty to protect him from exposure.

10.  NEVER DIE WHILE IN YOUR DOCTOR'S PRESENCE OR UNDER HIS
     DIRECT CARE.
        This will only cause him needless inconvenience and embarrassment.
