Subject: Weather's unnatural acts

I was once lucky enough to experience a tornado without being one of
the poor unfortunates in its path.   Tornado alerts are not that unusual
in Kentucky, but Louisville, being in somewhat of a shallow valley, was
usually spared.

I was out delivering newspapers one afternoon in 1974, and remember feeling
an erie stillness in the air.  I looked up to see what looked like a thick,
coal-black cloud tumbling overhead.  It was moving along pretty quickly and
didn't make a sound.

When I came home I learned that a tornado had touched down in several
places in the city, and fortunately had passed over our neighborhood.
I set out on my Honda to respond to an emergency call for blood donors,
and then roamed the city to inspect the aftermath first hand.

The destruction done by a tornado is awesome not only in its completeness,
but in its selectivity and the distinctness of the edges of its path.
Bare foundations sat next to houses without even a broken window.  The
neighborhoods hit looked as if a 200-foot wide Lawnboy rolled through them.

Cherokee Park used to be a couple of square miles of venerable old trees
and lush greenness, and was a place to "hang-out" in high school, and a
sanctuary in which to get lost from everything when you needed to be alone.
It was unsettling to see 300-year old trees disrespectfully scattered about
like jackstraws, and to realize that because of a mere five minutes, an old
refuge wouldn't be the same again in my lifetime.

From: CASTOR::J_COVERT         "23-Feb-1983 07:47"

Subject: Fourth Grade Teachers

Hurray to your fourth grade teacher for making you remember that it's
its that doesn't have the apostrophe, but she's wrong about the reason.

The lack of the apostrophe on the possessive pronoun "its" is NOT an
exception.  There's no apostrophe on any possessive pronoun: my, your,
his, her, its, our, your, and their.  However, when it's a contraction
of "it is" that you're dealing with, the apostrophe is used to indicate
the missing letters.

From: MILVAX::FRIBERG
To:   KERMIT::T_PARMENTER
Subject: About apostrophes--

  The possesive 'its' is the same type of pronoun as 'his' and 'hers'.
  The contraction 'it's' is really two words, 'it' and 'is'. They are
  not even remotely related.

  Also incorrect is the use of apostrophes in plurals such as
        "The Miller's". (Correct: The Millers)
  Use of the apostrophe here is incorrect. Apostrophes are not necessary
  for pluralization for most nouns. Cases where this is most unclear
  include acronyms and words already ending with s, but the same rules
  do apply.

  If you are talking about something that belongs to the Millers, the
  apostrophe goes after the 's', since it is assume that said article
  belongs to all the Millers. ("The Millers' boat...")

  I find that most often people use apostrophes where they do not belong
  rather than leave them out where they do belong. They are proper only
  to create contractions of two words and in possessive nouns. ('It' is
  a pronoun.)

From: METOO::ELUDOM::BOYD
To:   METOO::KERMIT::T_PARMENTER
Subject: its versus it's, once more with feeling

        Olde English nouns had cases galore, and several patterns
(declensions to the cognescenti) to follow.  Most of this went away,
and we are left with the merest remnants.  They sound like a base form,
or root, and the base + S-sound (sometimes a Z-sound).  The latter
indicates plurality, or possession, or both.  In conversation, you
can usually figure out what is meant.  In writing, though, you can't
ask the author to rephrase things.  Ye olde Scribes Guild figured out
punctuation to make this (and other things) clear.  The curve above
the word (that's what "apostrophe" means, from Greek roots) indicates
possession.
        Now the Pronouns persisted in all their glory, indicating
number, case, (and sometimes gender too), so there never was any need
to distinguish one form from another. Thus "its", "his", "hers"
don't need anything extra written down.  Sure, they have a final S,
and it does indicate possesion, but they are unique, unlike any other
form of the pronouns.
        (An aside: the Modern English pronouns do not derive from the
Old English pronouns.  They are the result of the Danish invasion,
before 1066 and all that, and they are more related to Old Norse
than to Old English.  An anomaly, since usually invaders don't
change such basic stuff as pronouns, numbers, prepositions.)
        Contractions came a little later, and spoiled the nice
parsing of written text. For example, "the army's winning,"
could be a complete sentence, with a parallel sentence expected next,
or it could be a noun phrase, with expectations of a digressionary
clause, followed by some verb.
        / Milt

And, for closers on this fascinating topic - "Dear Aunt Mary, Thank
you for the book about penguins.  It tells me more about penguins than
I want to know." - here is the very first rule of usage from Strunk's
"Elements of Style":

        1. Form the possessive singular of nouns by adding 's.

        Follow this rule whatever the final consonant.  Thus write,

                Charles's friend
                Burns's poems
                the witch's malice

        Exceptions are the possessives of ancient proper names in "-es"
        and "-is", the possessive "Jesus'" and such forms as "for
        conscience' sake", "for righteousness' sake".  But such forms as
        "Moses' Laws", "Isis' temple" are commonly replaced by

                the Laws of Moses
                the temple of Isis

Why I don't lose sleep when I find out that a minority of people don't
know how to tie their shoe laces:

I just love the perrenial stories about professors who are disgusted
that one kind of a student or another can't place locations on the map,
can't name his state senators, don't know where their appendix is, can't
name the ten commandments, can't name the nine planets, can't identify
the opening line of Moby Dick, or who can't whistle the opening phrase
of Beethoven's fifth.

Now, far be it from me to point out that none of these skills is of the
least importance to feeding, breeding or having a "lifestyle".  Far be it
from me to say that people who make their living knowing where Miami is
(airline pilots and football teams, that sort of thing) invariably KNOW
where Miami is because it is relevant to them.  Let's look at the sort
of professor who puts test like this together.  This is a man who is
hunting.

You don't just wake up in the morning and say, "I wonder how many people
know where Miami is?"  This professor is grinding an ax. 8.5 percent
of the people can't find Miami?  8.5 percent?  Really?  My, I'll bet
that makes the other 91.5% pretty smart.  I'll bet it makes the professor
pretty smart, because he found out.  Might even get his name in the
papers.  And certainly, it can't be his fault if a given course is a
flop, because this proves that SOMEONE ELSE in the educational chain
didn't do good job preparing the student.  Not him.  Not his job to
teach place names, n'est pas?

No need to worry about future mapmakers, politicians or travel agents.
Mapmakers CARE where places are, and will know.  Travel agents just
write out the tickets that get you to where you're going, and don't have
to know latitude and longitude.  (A travel agent who had never been to
the Bahamas told me where to find the most beautiful beach I have ever
seen.  She knew all about WHAT was there, but had no idea WHERE I was
going.)  Politicians are responsible for naming places. Places ARE where
they say.  Not only have they defined the boundaries of Miami, they have
CHANGED them several times.

I wonder what kind of numbers we'd come up with if we tried to find out
how many people can tie their own bow tie?  How many people know when
you wear a dinner jacket, and when you wear tails?  Where does the
dessert fork go in a place setting?  How do you conjugate the Latin verb
"sum"?

How do these things differ from the location of Miami?  They used to be
important, but aren't anymore.  The location of Miami is not important,
and never has been.
