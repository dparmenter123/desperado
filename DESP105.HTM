From: ATFAB::WYMAN

Subject: DIGICOMP-I

My first computer was also a DIGICOMP-I... I'd read the Sept 65 Scientific
American special issue on computing and decided that I wanted to play with
one of those toys, so my dad got me one for Christmas...

Well, I don't have it anymore, but I think of it often. You see, it was a neat
little box, and even came with some programming examples. It also had a long
section in its manual on "theory" which was understandable. The problem is
that it never gave you even the vaguest hint as to how to actually program it.
All you had was three (maybe four) sample programs, without comments. (One of
them simulated an elevator system...) I must have spent months trying to
figure the beast out. Finally, we sent a very nasty letter to Edmund Scientific
and got a reply back that they were getting together a new manual that actually
said how to program the thing and they would send us a free copy... It never
arrived.  Whenever I read some of DEC's better manuals I think of my
DIGICOMP-I. Sometimes I think the DIGICOMP-I documentation is an improvement
over some of what we ship... At least it had programming examples.

                bob wyman

From: PETTENGILL   "PRODUCT IS THE GOAL"
To:   KERMIT::PARMENTER,PETTENGILL
Subject: RE: #1092: I keep my fingernails long

As I mentioned earlier, I now have a different view on versions

                V1 is a necessity
                V2 is a sign of ingenuity or weakness
                V3 is a sign of failure

We're in the business of producing and selling products.  If we don't produce
them, we can't sell them.  Therefore V1.  Sometimes, we release V1 before its
ready in order to prepare the market.  Hence, V2.  But other times, we take
a perfectly reasonable product and one plus it because we don't have anything
better to do.

V3, and there have been many at DEC, is a real failure.  Usually we are trying
to develop some really new ideas.  But the process of starting a new product,
the reluctance to leave an old friend, the bullshit of product maintanence,
cause us to hide the new idea inside an old idea.  Bad news!

        o We don't gain the full benefit of a new product
        o We end up dealing with a lot baggage when we should just flat out
          tell the customer, if you want the stuff you've got to have the
          right hardware.  After all, we are in the business of selling stuff.
        o We end up giving the new stuff away to a large number of customers

Let's use VMS as an example.  V1 was bare bones.  V2 made it a real contender.
But V3 should have been called VMSplus V1 or something.  A new product.
V4 called VMSclusters V1.  Meanwhile MicroVMS V1 would have been created and
out by now.  Another new product.  I beleive that the directions that VMS has/is
going would have much clearer.

Much of my thinking here comes from notes from Ron Ham.  Just to show I'm not
biased, P/OS sure is stretching the point.  V1.0, V1.1, V1.5, V1.7, and V2.0.
And V3.0 is rumored to be coming in the fall.

||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||
This is really an eye-opening idea.

Go back and read it again.  Every strength is a weakness.  Samson's
hair made him strong, but Delilah cut it and it made him weak.  One of
DEC's great strengths is compatibility, but it's a weakness as well,
when we try to preserve the purity of every command procedure and
program that anyone might have ever written.  I think the RSX-11M
customers would not have minded if we'd left it alone starting four
years ago and worked only on RSX-11M-PLUS till we got that in pretty
good shape and then switched to Micro/RSX 18 months ago.  In some
ways, that's what happened anyway, but we didn't know it.

How would this idea apply to your product?
