 I celebrated the metastasis of Netscape by downloading the
 Quarterdeck Internet Suite (http://www.qdeck.com), which promises
 superior features to what they call "the blinking browser",
 particularly in URL management.

 To install Netscape I had to acquire Netscape, Trumpet Winsock,
 TIA, and four "helper"utilities and then engage a guru to link
 them all together.  This is not a three-billion-dollar product, it is
 a rinky-dink app that can't stand alone for a second.  Of course, to
 install the Quarterdeck Suite I need to find out something about a
 bunch of stuff I have only vaguely heard of, my "servers": POP, SMTP
 and NNTP, and plug 'em in to an installation procedure that tells me
 nothing about any of these things.

 Somebody, could be Bill Gates, but it need not be, could be you, dear
 reader, is going to put a browser together that installs, works, and
 does what we want it to.

 worth the money.  The services just won't be able to evolve fast
 enough and who needs im when you've got Yahoo? (Now there's a stock

> A(4) = 4 # 4 # 4 # 4 = 4 # 4 # (4 ^ 4 ^ 4 ^ 4)
>                      = 4 # 4 # (4 ^ 4 ^ 256)
>                      ~ 4 # 4 # (4 ^ (1.34 * 10 ^ 154))
>                      ~ 4 # 4 # (10 ^ (8.07 * 10 ^ 153))
>                      ~ 4 # (4 ^ 4 ^ ... (way more than a gogoolplex times))
>

I've seen this function before with the name WOW attached to it, as in
WOW(1)=1, WOW(2)=4, WOW(3)=7625597484987, etc.  It occasionally comes up in
discussions about growth rates of functions.  There are functions that
grow linearly, grow as some polynomial, and grow exponentially.  People
tend to think of exponential rates as the fastest kind of growth.  As
pointed out, exponential growth is only Order 3.  Things like # are Order
4, while functions like WOW are _infinite_ order, i.e. grow faster
than any Order n function for all n.

Of course, in one sense it's a mistake to think of this as really fast; as
anyone who has studied Set Theory to the point of learning about
inaccessible cardinals can tell you, this isn't even the tip of the
iceberg.  On the other hand, if one searches for meaning in mathematics
(sometimes--but not always--recommended), it's going too far.  I've yet to
see a "real-life" significance for Order 4 functions much less things like
WOW.  I definitely like to ponder such things, but I usually label them
mathematical philosophy.
