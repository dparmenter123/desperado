Subj:   A tale of postal woe

A while back, on a trip to Washington I bought a packet of self-adhesive
stamps.  Instead of using glue the stamps were pressed onto a plastic
sheet so you could peel them off when you needed them. They are great for
wallets because the glue isn't there to make the stamps stick together.
Little bit pricey $5 for 18 stamps but we gotta pay for technology right?

I return home and run out of these stamps. Go down to the local post
office for another batch. Bad idea. Turns out that the stampos are a test
market in some 12 cities. Boston isn't one of them.

So I call the post office stamp by phone service 1 800 stamp 24. You can
order stamps by visa or mastercard this way. First off they want my credit
card number followed by my name and address, zip code. Then they ask me
for my state and city. Now they are the post office - don't they have
access to the zip code listing? Then it turns out that they only sell
regular stamps and not this special item.

Then I remember that I had picked up a catalog  for stamp collectors to
order stamps by mail. They do  sell the self-adhesive stamps by mail. You
can  also call them and order stamps by phone (payment by credit card).
Call them up and go through the  same questions including the state and
city. They don't hjave their computers tied into the zip code database
either! "We're just not that advanced sir," said  the clerk, who was
extremely helpful nonetheless.

Now I'm not asking for the postal service to have  advanced telemarketing
with incoming caller identification so they can poick up my name and
automatically  retrieve my file from the database (assuming that I am a
regular customer). But it would be be nice if they could pick up the zip
code info and make a stab at guessing my location- it would give us
cuystomers some faith that the post office is trying to do the best that
it can.

To top it all off I went to my local post office  to ask for another
stamp - a hologram of a space shuttle which is embossed ont an envelope.
Costs 30 cents (it's a 25 cent stamp). The  local clerks had heard about
it but the only people who had them were some clerks who are only on duty
during the week (this was on a Saturday) I didn't know that buying stamps
was a personal service!

    Date: Wed, 14 Mar 90 20:49 EST
    From: Steve Strassmann <straz@media-lab.media.mit.edu>
    To: unix-haters@ai.ai.mit.edu
    Subject: [drewry@decwrl.dec.com: what to do with a PDP-11/73]

    From: drewry@decwrl.dec.com (Raymond Drewry)
     From: Michel Jackson <jackson@shs.ohio-state.edu>
      From: karl@giza.cis.ohio-state.edu (Karl Kleinpaste)
      Newsgroups: osu.hacks
      Subject: How do you measure nuclear warhead yield?

    This is something I learned at the USENIX Conference in January that
    I've been meaning to post here, but have managed to forget about until
    now.

    While chatting with some network acquaintances at the hotel bar (all
    the important discussion occurs at the bar, of course, preferably well
    past midnight), a friend who does sysadmin work at Los Alamos National
    Labs told us a marvelously funny story about how the fun folks at LANL
    measure yield from nuclear detonations.  After all, they have to
    experiment, I guess, and one has to learn how much bang-for-the-Mbuck
    one is getting.

    The solution at LANL (note that this is now an 8-week-old memory,
    details may be somewhat inaccurate):

    Find a Qbus-based PDP-11 (e.g., 11/73) "which you no longer love."
    Install a DEQNA ethernet controller card in the backplane.  Park the
    box at/near/over the hole.  Connect a cable to the DEQNA and drop it
    down into the hole.

    DEQNAs have a TDR (time domain reflectometer) built right into the
    controller.  TDR is useful for finding cable shorts and, in general,
    learning the length of one's ethernet cable.

    Before detonation, begin having the PDP-11 repeatedly exercise the
    DEQNA's TDR, recording and transmitting the length determined to some
    other (presumably distant :-) site.

    Detonate.  As the beastie blows things to smithereens all around
    itself, the cable will be rapidly eaten away.  TDR readings from the
    DEQNA will show a drastically reducing cable length.  The speed with
    which the cable, ah, degenerates will correlate very closely with
    warhead yield.

    Just think, your tax dollars at work, ridding the world of PDP-11s...

    --karl

    PS- No, I'm not kidding.  Not a word of it.

Hi.  This is so funny, I had to share it with you.  It ought to be part of
a Monty Python act!

The story: I'm trying to figure out some Motif behavior stuff.  My "source"
is a document called the Motif Style Guide Technical Specification.  Reading
it is like wading through mud!  It's ugly!  It's technical!  It's full of
jargon!

So, to get some help I wrote up some questions and sent them to Ellis Cohen at
OSF.  Ellis wrote the technical spec, so I was going straight to the horse's
mouth, so to speak.

I told Ellis that I was confused about the difference between a "field" and
a "group".  This is what he wrote back... (brace yourself!)

I wrote: "First, I'm a bit confused on the exact definition of a Field versus a
Group."

His reply:

"A group is a component that has subcomponents.
A control is a component that has no subcomponents.

Some controls and some groups are fields.  Some are not.

Field controls are fields.  Other controls are not fields.

Some groups are automatically fields -- in particular,
   basic groups, dialogue boxes, and the top-level group in a window.
Other groups are not made fields automatically , although a client can
   explicitly set any group to be a field.

If a field is a control, then traversing to a field simply traverses to
   that control.
If a field is a group, then traversing to a field traverses to some
   control in the field -- the default controls if there is one,
   otherwise the top-left most one (in a l to r language environment)

Be careful.  Since groups may be nested, fields may be nested too.
When you traverse to such a field, you will land on a control
   that is in that field and not in a field nested within it."

Oh!  It's all perfectly clear now! :-)
