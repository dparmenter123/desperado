subject: googols and googolplexes

someone even more mathematically pedantic than i am will probably
have mentioned this already, but just in case...

   From: tompar@world.std.com (Tom Parmenter)

   The googol is "a one with 100 zeroes after it", that is, "10 to the
   hundredth power".  A googolplex is "a googol multiplied times itself a
   googol times", that is, "a googol to the googolth power".

The googolplex has always been one of my favorite "weird 'n huge"
numeric figures, so I cannot stand to see it misquoted. :-)

A googolplex is 10 to the power of a googol:

          100
        10
      10

The number you specify might be called an "exponentiated" googol,
or a googolgoogol:

                (10 ^ 100)
      (10 ^ 100)

now for really huge (though still picayune compared with aleph 0),
try the "exponentiated" googolplex, or googolplexgoogolplex:

                       (10 ^ (10 ^ 100))
      (10 ^ (10 ^ 100))

this is best taken with a large glass of water a googol hours
before eating.

In the world of competitive charcoal-lighting, "pretty good" does not
cut the mustard.  Thus, Goble hit upon the idea of using - get ready -
liquid oxygen.  This is the form of oxygen used in rocket engines;
it's 295 degrees below zero and 600 times as dense as regular oxygen.
In terms of releasing energy, pouring liquid oxygen on charcoal is the
equivalent of throwing a live squirrel into a room containing 50
million Labrador retrievers.  On Gobel's World Wide Web page (the
address is http://ghg.ecn.purdue.edu/), you can see actual photographs
and a video of Goble using a bucket attached to a 10-foot-long wooden
handle to dump 3 gallons of liquid oxygen (not sold in stores) onto a
grill containing 60 pounds of charcoal and a lit cigarette for
ignition.  What follows is the most impressive charcoal-lighting I
have ever seen, featuring a large fireball that, according to Goble,
reached 10,000 degrees Fahrenheit.  The charcoal was ready for cooking
in - this has to be a world record - 3 seconds.
