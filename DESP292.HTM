To sleep, perchance to dream... (1)

To go, perchance never to return.

Yes, the unknown beckons, seducing you with the idea that it has something
better, something more interesting, something more exciting and just plain
something MORE!

But as you get to know an unknown, it no longer remains the alluring unknown,
but becomes the commonplace.  Remember, your known is someone else's unknown
and vice versa.  You've learned something new, you've seen something you hadn't
before, you've gained an experience.  All very worthy.  But you've lost
something for possible discovery, you've lost a future opportunity and worst,
you've lost the dream of what might be, because you now know what is.

I heard this song when I was a child:  (Forgive the paraphrase and the awkward
translation, but although I believe the original is in English, I only ever
heard it in German.)

Even as a child, I knew
That to always be in one place
Was too much for me to bear.
I read books day and night.
And always dreamed,
That one day I would be
Five thousand miles from my home....

And as our hero grew up he really had the opportunity to travel and found
himself to be "Five thousand miles from my home...."  And he saw that wherever
he travelled he found wonder and excitement.  Everywhere the world was a
beautiful place.  And he enjoyed being "Five thousand miles from my home...."
And then one day he wanted to return to his home, and he found that the place
had changed.  It was not as he remembered it.  And he realized that even here
he was "Five thousand miles from my home...."

I liked this song because it had a catchy tune.  It wasn't until recently that
I listened to the words and saw my life in them.  My mother is German.  My
father, Austrian.  They met in Switzerland and now live in New Jersey.  I am
married to a Brit.  Our son has three citizenships.  I've lived in Michigan,
Germany, New Jersey, Austria and England.  My 'hometown' is Vienna, Austria,
one of the most beautiful European capitals I know.  I love it and feel strong
ties to it.  Every time I visit, I am just that, a visitor.  My relatives take
me sightseeing and introduce me to their friends as "the niece from America",
although I no longer reside in the States.  My parents are about to retire and
plan to return to Austria.  They are terrified of the changes that reshaped
this home of theirs over the last quarter of a century, and wonder if they can
cope.  I love visiting Vienna, but feel a definite pang and hurt at being
treated as a foreigner.  Wherever I am, I am accustomed to being just a little
different to my neighbours.  I don't mind it, even enjoy it a bit.  But every
once in a while, I'd like to go home.

(1)    (Some bloke named Shakesbeer said that in his famous play "Omlet", or
was it "Ham and Eggs".)
