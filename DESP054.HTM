Subject: Origin of name TRON

I've heard what I believe to be a more likely source for the name TRON.

TRON is a PDP-10 instruction (Test Right, Ones, and skip if Not all masked
bits equaled 0).  It's opcode happens to be 666, the number of the beast.
It is known that PDP-10s were used to generate much of the graphic sequences
in the movie.

There is also a duplication of a computer "readout" in the book that is taken
directly from a TOPS-10 completion message.  (There's a rumor that the author
asked some of the special effects people for some "computerese" to include in
portion of the book and that is what they gave him.)

From: RUNE::B_TODD           "11-Mar-1983 04:15"
To:   PARMENTER,'CILLA
Subject: STRATUS comments

Thanks to both of you (I already thanked Rick) for the STRATUS notes.  The
'how many backup units are we shipping with MICRO' heading may be a bit
unfair to us:  with 8 $100 processors in their box, not to mention the
cost of all the other duplicated hardware, and ESPECIALLY not to mention
the cost of duplicating disks, I don't think STRATUS will EVER be competing
in the same market as MICRO, though with volume to soak up the software
costs they might eventually get systems down into the $40K - $50K range.
By definition, wholly-redundant ('non-stop') systems suffer a price/
performance disadvantage factor of at least 2 up to the point where the
costs of the terminals (assumed non-redundant) swamp the cost of the rest
of the (redundant) hardware - and terminal prices are still going down.

Fail-safe operation is a fascinating technical problem, and the STRATUS
approach seems pleasingly clean, but in the real world there seem to be
very few people willing to pay twice as much for their computer just to
gain the difference between reliability and infallibility (despite our
fears, Tandem didn't take over the world after all).  These few people
have system needs in the mid-range and up:  the V-11 seems to be the
lowest-cost processor we have in sight to which this type of system
would be applicable (and I hope someone is working on one).  Low-end
systems should still concentrate on price/performance, and provide as
much reliability (and easy recovery from failure) as they can without
driving up hardware cost.
                                        - Bill
