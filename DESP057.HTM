                       GOING OUT IN STYLE?

From: GALAXY::MURRAY
To:   KERMIT::T_PARMENTER
Subject: Jon Parsons' query (Desperado #1038)

Jon Parsons wondered why people will use "as if it were going
out of style" to refer to something's popularity, whereas logic
seems to call for the opposite interpretation. The usage may
simply indicate user carelessness and ignorance, but there's
another possibility that occurs to me:

Whenever the great mass of people becomes enthusiastic about
something (an investment scheme, fashion, fad, etc.), that's
an almost sure sign that the thing is on the decline or is about
to begin its decline. Thus, for instance, if "they're buying widgits
like they are going out of style," then the odds are that,
unbeknownst to the naive panic-buyers, the popular fascination
with widgits is about to end.

From: KIRK::CROLL
To:   PARMENTER
Subject: the U.S. answer to Japan?

The March 24, 1983 issue of Electronics has an article about the formation
of something called the Microelectronics & Computer Technology Corp.,
headed by Bobby R. Inman.  The MCC was formed to give U. S. companies a
means of sharing research and development costs without running afoul of
antitrust regulations.  It has an operating budget of $50 to $85 million
yearly, and will do R&D that its sponsors will then try to turn into
products.

The initial shareholders in the new corporation are:  DEC, Mostek, RCA,
Sperry, Harris, NCR, Motorola, Control Data, Honeywell, National
Semiconductor, and Advanced Micro Devices.

One of the projects mentioned in the article is one that will last ten
years with a team of 80 to 90 engineers and scientists.  It consists of a
computer architecture effort that MCC calls Alpha/Omega.  Its goal is the
development and transfer of selective technologies for future generations
of  computers and smart peripherals.  It has four parts:

        * an artificial-intelligence effort to establish the theoritical
          groundwork for prototype AI architectures and to deliver a
          prototype system capable of 100,000 "inferences" a second.

        * a prototype database machine architecture with a tenfold
          performance improvement over present processing architectures

        * a new user interface incorporating a prototype speech- and image
          recognition system

        * one or more parallel processing architectures.

According to the article, the MCC is widely assumed to be the U. S.
response to Japan's Ministry of International Trade and Industry,
responsible for much of that country's operative research in computers.

For more details, refer to the article:  Electronics, March 24, 1983, pg.
89.

     The doubts CACHE::BUCH expresses about the concrete meaning of 'the whole
nine yards' leave ME unconvinced.  As he says <<roughly>>: 'the mixer of
a concrete truck [was] big . . . [even back when I was] eight years old.'
After all, a sphere with 9 cubic yards volume has a radius of only 1.29 yards,
or 7'8'' diameter.  As the mixer on a cement truck is elongated from
sphericity, its maximum diameter would be even smaller.  As a casual admirer
of cement trucks (without benefit of technical specifications) for upwards
of three decades, I am doubtful that creatures of this size are such a
recent innovation.  Also, we are reliably informed that the smallest
truck in current general use is 8 cubic yards.  I find it hard to swallow
the SMALLEST now to be seen exceeds the capacity of the LARGEST to be
found a mere 15 years ago.  I suggest invocation of those hard paternal data.

     As to GALAXY::PARSONS's concern about garbled similes, 'selling X
like it was going out of style' would indeed make no sense.  The
correct idiom is: 'They [or he, she] were SPENDING MONEY like it was
going out of style.'  The sense is clear.  Curious how it got turned
inside out while being passed along.

The Great Salt Lake in Utah (as with many large lakes) goes through
long term fluctuations in depth that significantly affect its size.
Although currently smaller than it was in the 19th Century, the Lake
is currently in an expansion phase that has lasted over a decade.

In recent years this current expansion began seriously to threaten
buildings, factories, roads, and other facilities built near the
now approaching shoreline.  Four years ago, the Utah legislature passed
a bill making it illegal for the Lake to exceed a height of 4202 feet
above mean sea level.  As of February, 1983, the Lake inched above
the proscribed level, making it the nation's largest known outlaw.

The State has not yet tried to prosecute the Lake, and it is not clear
what form of punishment would fit the crime.  Life imprisonment seems
ambiguous at best.  Banishment to forced labor in the salt mines
seems impractical.  Perhaps they will simply have to resort to a
modified form of Chinese Water Torture.
