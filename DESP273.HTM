To: desperado@world.std.com
From: "Fred R. Goldstein" <fgoldstein@BBN.COM>
Subject: Boys will be boys, browsers browsers

We former users of Cutler's pre-Bill operating systems can appreciate your
comments on Unix, the operating system that denies its masculinity.  Oddly
enough, I posted comments not entirely unlike yours to one of the Linux
newsgroups.  They were discussing what to do next, and I suggested a Linux
that was as friendly as a DOS shell but not as stupid.  And thanks to the
wonders of symbolic links, it could have both a straightforward,
descriptively-titled directory structure _and_ the traditional filenames
that U-boys love.  Needless to say the only responses were flamage, as if I
had proposed, say, that Mecca host a Billy Graham tent meeting.

But I think I know why.  Ever notice who wears those T-shirts with the
daemons on them?  Unix is a demographic play.  Just as Star Trek and Matlock
don't share much audience, Unix belongs to a demo-group.   Mostly, it's
males born after 1956.

It's not old folks, though of course there are the wizened gray-bearded
exeptions who tell tales of Version 5 and who even may have seen code
written in B.  No, the older generation of computer geeks got started
elsewhere and probably stayed elsewhere.  Unix hit MIT in the early '70s and
spread under the tutelage of its evangelist, Get-em Young,  who gave free
copies to universities, when DEC actually expected some of them to pay money
for software.  A generation was born, not Gen X but Gen C.

And it's male.  Sure, so's computing in general, but Unix is special.  Its
appeal is to the boys who were too scholarly or just geeky to join the
frats.  But even geeks and nerds want the trappings of membership, the
secret handshakes, the code language. And that's what makes Unix so much
fun.  The obscurity is the appeal.  Once you've immanetized the man, cat and
grep, you feel like you've joined.  It's a frat for geeks, complete with
conventions, magazines, and a really good alumni job network.  I don't think
females have quite as much attraction to such clubs, but in any case
Unix seems to be a boy's frat.

How can you make Unix friendly to the other 99% of computer users without
spoiling it?  That would be like turning the Shriners into the Jaycees.
Exclusivity
is a plus for a private club.  I watched the reactions when the FCC proposed
dropping the Morse Code exam from one class of Ham Radio license, and it was
awful.  Once you've earned that ham ticket, you're a member of an exclusive
frat, and that silly old code is as much a part of the initiation rite today
as it was for grandpa.  The FCC was, to many, a party-pooper by opening it
up on the basis of a more relevant set of exams.  Same thing, only Unix has
peer pressure rather than the FCC to keep it pure..

Speaking of clubs, it dawned on me why the Smith family opened up
Cybersmith.  Back in the old days their original bookstore in Harvard
Square, Paperback Booksmith, prominently displayed its slogan, "Dedicated to
the Fine Art of Browsing".  That store is long gone, but nowadays they
charge seventeen and a half cents to use a browser.  Ah, progress.

original claim for keeping the OS and the hardware closed was that

inculcate the same attitude in its users as seems to have been held by
its creators/proliferators: not RTFM, but "RTF code".  Most of the
systems I've seen (cross-) developed on Unix platforms lack any
serious design documentation, and the user documentation is often far
removed from the reality.

While you mentioned memory, you didn't really go into that major change in
the world.  RSX actually was useful with as little as 32 kilobytes of memory
and that was multi-_tasking_.  To be useful supporing multiple users or heavy
personal use you needed memory management and that meant systems that were
much larger, say on the order of 128 KB.  But that was a pig compared to
RSTS (no /E) that supported 25 uesrs with 28KW or 56KB.

A few months ago as I was recalling our glory years in RSX, I thought about
the memory, the disk, and what we thought were useful minimums relative to
the sales and marketing types.

That thought lead me to begin thinking about computer systems in terms of
the years.  Rather describing the computer we just bought, we just say
"I just bought a system with the capacity of every system in 1960."
"Yeah, why so small, I bought a 1963 sixth months ago."

The problem I have is finding the data on computer systems, memory, and disk
sold by year....

I have a theory about why UNIX is the way that it is.

        It is hard to understand ... on purpose.

For the most part the whole thing has the feel of a midnight hack,
grown out of control.  Clearly, marketing has never had anything to do
with the shell commands.  So where did the names come from?  Sotware
engineers thought them up, and they are too busy being creative to
stop and be consistent.  Also, since it apparently grew from a hack,
there was never anything with which to be consistent.

So, now it is a mess.  Who would drive the change?  Software
engineers, except they have no incentive.  Also, they *like* it like
this.  It is one *huge* puzzle.  It is at times incomprehensible.  It
often takes a ton of figuring out.

There is an unlimited amount of "getting better at it" possible.  This
is the sort of thing a high quality problem solver *loves*.  So, the
very people who would push to change it, love it the way it is
(subconciously for most) too much to change it.

Which leaves it a mess, and thus unsuitable for society at large.
