Greater Boston Chapter of the ACM, in a blurb signed by George Ballester:

        It is reported that IBM has the 370-on-a-chip as an add-on
        option for the IBM Personal Computer.  It is alleged that

an IBM PC for $5000.  You buy two very large disk drives (to hold the

the software license.  Assume the 370 option for the PC has
negligible cost (silicon is cheap, right?).  Presumably, somebody
will build an interface to slow the disks and memory down so the PC can
handle it.  (I wonder if you need a card reader, and punch?)

From: CASTOR::J_COVERT
To:   PARMENTER
Subject: Call letters

Although call letters for each country are established by the CCIR (the
International Radio Consultative Committee) (part of the ITU), in most
of the world they are not used by Broadcast Stations, but only by amateurs.

Most stations in Europe, the Middle East, Africa, and Asia identify them-
selves by announcing their station name and location.  Most domestic
stations in North America and the Pacific (including Australia and New
Zealand) identify by using a callsign of 3 to 5 letters or a combination
of letters and numbers, of which the first one or two letters denote the
nationality of the station in accordance with the international system of
prefixes.  Most stations in Latin America use a callsign (often not freq-
uently announced), a station name or slogan, and location.

In addition, many stations (especially international ones) use interval
signals generally consisting of musical phrases repeated frequently before
sign-on and between programmes, to help listeners to find the station on
their receiver, and to identify it as the wanted station.

For example, the Voice of America does not use call letters, but does
use two bars of Yankee Doodle at the beginning and conclusion of trans-
missions and within programs.

And then, as a Ramblin' WREK from Georgia Tech, our station had appropriate
call letters.

(more or less).  The idea is for heavily populated areas (like NYC) to have

From: REX::LEICHTERJ
To:   ALIEN::PARMENTER ! SENT TO ALIEN::PARMENTER
Subject: codes

Area codes were assigned at a time when must signalling was by the old "click"
(make-break connection) method.  The more clicks required for a signal, the
longer to set up a call.  Sending "1" takes 2 clicks; "2" is 3; and so on up
to "0", which is 11 clicks.  So...the more populous areas got the "low-click"
area codes:  New York City gets the shortest (212, 7 clicks); Los Angeles is
213, Chicago 312 - 8 clicks a piece; and so on.  (Salt Lake City, as I recall
from my time there, is 801 - actually, that's all of Utah.  A look at the phone
book reveals that "Bermuda, Puerto Rico, Virgin Islands, and the Bahamas"
share 809 - a prefix I remember dialing from the Mill when I talked to the
Puerto Rican plants regularly.  They, however, may have gotten stuck with a
late assignment.)

This assignment is obvious once it's pointed out, but surprisingly hard to
find on your own - certainly until you notice that "0" is "big", not "small".
                                                        -- Jerry

From: STAR::LIONEL
To:   ALIEN::PARMENTER
Subject: Area Code Madness

Area codes are very definitely assigned according to a system.  First of
all, the middle digit has to be 0 or 1.  Once that's taken care of, the areas
with the most frequently dialed numbers get the codes with the fastest to
dial (not touch!) digits.  NYC with 212 is the tops, with 213 (LA) and
312 (Chicago) right behind. Eastern Mass with 617 isn't too bad, but areas
with a middle digit of 0 (like 603!) are lower-caste.  The worst I can
find is 809 (Bermuda, etc.), but a 909 would top that.

Of course, with Touch-Tone (tm) dialing, it makes a lot less difference.

From: METOO::BRADLEY
To:   ALIEN::PARMENTER
Subject: pattern in area codes

area codes were assigned when dialing meant turning a dial, rather than
pushing a button.  the area codes are assigned so the most frequently called
areas (called from outside the area) have the least total finger travel.
thus:  new york=212, chicago=312, los angeles=213, alaska=907.
the middle digit is 1 or 0.  there are many holes in the sequence.
also, the order was an estimate of the relative frequency of calls over
some time period in the future, probably 40 or 50 years.

if you find it hard to believe ma bell could be that considerate,
remember you are not paying them while you dial.  you might spend a little
more time on the phone if you spend less time dialing.

all of the above is conjecture.  i asked the same question many years ago
but never confirmed the answer.

From: KHOTEK::BINDER
To:   ALIEN::PARMENTER
Subject: RE: Worth the wait?

The frosting on raisin bread (ah, how well I remember it!) fell victim to two
pernicious trends in American society:

1.  There was an outcry some years ago from the mothers of America against all
    the sugar that was just slathered on everything.  In the case of sweet
    rolls and other obvious delights, nothing was done, as the very reason for
    those things to exist is the sugar.  But raisin bread is/was supposedly a
    nutritious source of starch, vitamins, and other yecch; the fact that it
    tastes really good is/was serendipity (even raisins are GOOD for ya!)  So
    exit the frosting, which was nothing more than a sugar glaze.

2.  The second, and real reason, is that it cost too much for the barons of
    industry to stomach, so they are saving megabucks by not putting it on.

The tragedy of all this is that the major constituent of most breakfast cereals
is still sugar (53% in Apple Jacks), but it is hidden, and it thereby escapes
notice for the most part.  Note:  the expense that was cut in raisin bread was
not the sugar itself but the processing to apply it.

Have you ever watched (listened to) cereal commercials carefully?  The voice-
over ALWAYS declaims that Toasti-pops or whatever "is a part of this nutritious
breakfast."  Of course the breakfast is nutritious - it contains fresh fruit,
ham or bacon, milk, and OJ as a minimum.  Sometimes they also include toast and
jelly.  The cereal plays virtually no part in the overall benefit, but the Feds
can't cite the commercial as fraud, because there are no lies.
