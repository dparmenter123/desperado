Yet more on Indians:

As anyone who phantoms the remainder tables knows, Willard Espy is the
last word on words.  Mr. Espy turns out volume after volume of etymology
for the millions, and as far as I know has never once sold one for list
price.  If you like to read about word origins, you might look for some
of his books in second hand book stores or the three for a dollar table
of you favorite bookseller.

All this leads up to Espy on Indians.  According to friend Willard, the
term Indian Giver started with a curious custom where if an Indian gave
you a gift, he would take it back if you did not give him one in return.
Marketeers do a similar thing with business cards.  Indian Sign supposedly
refers to a hand gesture that Indians used to ward off evil.  Probably
like the Italians in the North End of Boston use to this day.

Another bit of word trivia which I bring up every year about this time
deals with the verb "to snow" and the value of a Boston education.
The school board in the city of Boston feels that for an education to be
good, it must be properly archaic.  Therefore, anyone from Boston knows
"to snow" is like the verb "to know" and runs "snow snew snown."  This
knowlege has limited value, since if you go around saying "It snew last
night" you'll only get a reputation as an eccentric.  On the other hand,
every time you hear someone say "It snowed last night" you'll cringe.

One answer is that people do spell privilege with whatever vowels come
to hand, and yet, we always know what they mean.  Even Japanese hi-fi
instructions are completely understandable.  Naturally, I don't write
that way, and neither does DELPHI::ELFSTROM.  We write Standard
American English the best we know how, but meanwhile, all around us,
like it or not, nonwriters are trying to turn "irregardless" into a
word.  Sometimes it works and sometimes it doesn't.  "Ain't" was
slangy and casual when it first appeared in English 400 years ago and
it's still slangy and casual.  "Mob" was slangy and casual when it
appeared in the language a couple of hundred years ago, and careful
writers refused to use it, but it is now perfectly good language. "Mob"
is an abbreviation of the Latin phrase mobile vulgus, meaning "the
movable common people" and I guess the objection of the careful
writers was that it wasn't their movability that was objectionable,
but their vulgarity.  Perhaps they would have accepted the term
"vulg".  "Decimate" used to mean "kill every tenth man" and it was a
punishment inflicted on disgraced Roman legions, but now it means
"kill a lot of them".

Furthermore, I don't believe in parts of speech, or sentence diagrams.

From: SPIDER::COLLIER

Subject: Babbage and music

Babbage did indeed fulminate extensively, and more than crankily, against
street musicians.  This is now usually perceived as simple eccentricity,
but his views were fairly widely shared by his contemporaries, who argued
that these musicians were a geunuine public nuisance.  Imagine that people
wandered around the corridors near your office all day playing obnoxious
music on the accordion (or substitute some other instrument you dislike),
and expecting you to toss quarters to them.  Babbage's London was not
characterised by large hermetically sealed buildings that isolated people
from street noise.

It is indeed true that Babbage could be cranky or worse, in letters and
elsewise, when he thought others were being pig-headed, small-brained,
or self-serving.  This was aften highly useful.  His 'Decline of Science
in England . . .', for example, was pure crankiness to the nth degree,
directed at a moribund Royal Society; its publication was an important
instrument in the reform movement that led to the founding of the British
Association for the Advancement of Science (model in turn for AAAS).  It
is also true that toward the end of his life crankiness played a somewhat
larger and more self-sustaining role in his behavior, due to disappointments
large and small, and simply to old age.  This is when street musicians
became prevalent, and the relevant letters were penned.

As to his reaction to synthesizers, it would most likely have been
complete disregard.  Music seems to have interested him not at all,
and it is one of the few areas of human endeavor to which he made no
significant contribution, though he is now remembered almost entirely
for his most stunning feat, the invention of the general purpose digital
computer.  In practical terms he was far more influential in other ways:
as a pioneer of scientific organization, operations research, statistical
analysis of public affairs, and a rational actuarial basis of the
life insurance industry.  He and a few peers helped pull British mathematics
out of the 17th century.  And he contributed to myriad other areas as
diverse as the introduction of artificial theatrical lighting and the
invention of coded occultations for lighthouses so that they could
be identifiable from ships at sea.  Lastly, he was by no means the
eccentric recluse people sometimes picture (again, somewhat excepting
the end of his life).  During his middle years, he was a great social lion,
and invitations to his huge weekly parties were among the most sought-after
in London.

Finally, 'ere the flame dies, Ada Augusta, Countess of Lovelace, was NOT
a programmer in any sense at all (let alone the first), and made NO
contribution to the design of the Analytical Engine or the construction
of algorithms with which it would solve problems.  Rather she was
quite successfully used by Babbage to publicize his machine, and he
was determined (for complex reasons) not to publish anything under his
own name.  He did not even meet Ada until nearly a decade after the
invention of the A.E., and she played a very small role in his life.
If you want to see cranky letters, read some of hers.  She was plagued
by illness, and on medical instruction often remained high on both
morphine and brandy.  She had real gambling problems, but they had
no connection to Babbage.  Her life, in short, would far more suitably
be written up in the National Enquirer than in the Transactions of the ACM.
She also did not invent 'bells and whistles'.

My candidate for the official DESPERADO dictionary is "Chamber's."  It is
the semi-standard dictionary for serious word game and puzzle fans, probably
because of its complement of obscure, archaic, and Scottish words as well as
its compact size and reasonable cost.  A very good choice if you like the
British-style cryptic crossword puzzles, as I do.

                ---------------------------------------

The phrase you parodied a recent DESPERADO, "When guns are outlawed, only
outlaws will have guns", has always struck me as one of those meaningless,
tautological phrases.  Clearly if guns are outlawed, every gun owner is then
an outlaw and hence only outlaws will have guns.

Of course, advertising is a rich source of words and phrases that seem to mean
something but don't.  One that always irritates me is usually delivered in a
pompous and stentorian manner, "BUY <product-name>, IT MAY BE THE MOST
IMPORTANT THING YOU DO TODAY."  I generally reply, calmly and quietly, "And
then again, maybe not."

An common advertising tautology is "at participating dealers". In other words,
"You can get it where you can get it and where you can't, you can't".
"Void where prohibited" is in the same class. Yet another is "nothing is better
than <product-name>"; translation, "we're no better than the rest."

                ---------------------------------------

Your use of the word "matriculate" reminded me of a form of word-play that
combines politics, logophilia and double-entendre. I refer to what I call the
"Smather", a political attack consisting of a factually correct statement,
incorporating a somewhat obscure word, phrased in such a way as to give an
erroneous impression to someone unfamiliar with the word.

For example, one politician alluded to his opponent's sister "who was a
thespian up in New York City."  A university was attacked by saying that
it was a place where "boys and girls matriculated together" and where "a girl
was required to show a professor her thesis."  (The were many more of these
university oriented ones; I wish I could remember the others.)

The name honors (?) Ex-senator George Smathers of Florida who is popularly
credited with making statements like these.  How many are really his and how
many were made up by journalists in Washington bars, I don't know.

Mike.
