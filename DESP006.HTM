                         FLINT VERSUS DENT

||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||

From: AURORA::GRODER
To:   ALIEN::PARMENTER
Subject: Indian corn

I don't know much about autumn leaves' color, but I have worked on a farm
for a few years and know a bit about Indian corn. Indian corn is a type of
flint corn. The other kind is called dent corn. The difference is in how
the kernels dry; flint corn (e.g. Indian corn and popcorn) dry from the
outside in, producing a hard (flint-like) covering, while dent corn (sweet
corn, ensilage corn) dries from the inside out (more or less),
causing the kernels to dent, or wrinkle up.

You can pop Indian corn and eat it (I've done it), but it doesn't taste all
that good, and about half of the kernels won't pop.

The colors just grow that way, you don't have to do anything to them.
Multiple ears on one stalk are all of the same color and pattern. I don't
know the science of how or why they get that way. As George Lamontagne
observed on the subject of leaves, don't worry about the science, just
enjoy 'em.

Indian corn has not been extensively hybridized; it is known as
open-pollinated. This means that you can plant the seeds from your Indian
corn next year and get equally good Indian corn the next fall. This is
*only if* the farmer didn't plant another variety of corn (such as sweet
corn) near it. Indian corn will cross pollinate with any other type of
corn, and the results of the cross are unpredictable.

TRUE STORY:

My brother also worked on this particular farm, and they used to have a
booth at the Topsfield Fair, selling cider and pumpkins and Indian corn,
etc.  He was working the booth one evening when three middle-aged ladies
approached, deep in discussion. They hung around in front of the booth for
about a minute before one of the women stepped up, pointed to the Indian
corn, and asked "How do they get those beautiful colors?".

Kurt replied, "It's genetic - they naturally grow that way."

"No, really, how do you do it?"

"Honest, they just grow that way."

The woman and her companions walked off, and the one woman said to her
friends "I KNEW he wouldn't tell me..."

Why is a native North American bird named the same as a country in
Asia?

It seems that just prior to the colonization of New World, the English
people were fond of eating a game bird that looked something like a
large Grouse. This bird had been brought to England from the "East" and
since at the time, everthing East of France was "Turkey" the bird was
nicknamed "Turkey Bird."

When the settlers arrived in the Colonies they discovered that the
Native Americans had obviously visited the land East of France because
they were fond of a similar game bird.

This discovery lent more credibility to the belief that Columbus had actually
found China.

Here in Seattle we know the origin of the bird's name because the natives
here refer to everyone living East of Spokane as turkeys. Especially those in
the other Washington.
