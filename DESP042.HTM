Ever feel oppressed by a programming language?

If programming languages were nations, their governments might
look something like this.

FORTRAN A fascist anarchy with few natural resources but considerable
        military might.
COBOL   A bureaucratic oligarchy which propogandized its dull citizenry.
ALGOL   A monarchy which divided rulership into a hierarchy of smaller
        political units; but lacked sophisticated social institutions.
APL     A small nation with advanced science, but primitive government,
        transportation, and services.  Because APL's currency was
        incompatible with that used by the rest of the world, trade was
        very restricted.
PL/1    A large dictatorship with military backing but unable to obtain
        popular support for its needlessly complex policies.
SNOBOL  A widely scattered society of highly literate citizens in the arctic.
LISP    A utopian anarchy with very little infrastructure.
POP-2   A colony of ALGOL which attempted to subjugate immigrants from
        LISP and SNOBOL.
BASIC   An oppressive dictatorship which easily took over many developing
        nations due to an absence of alternatives.
PASCAL  A bureaucratic state, borrowing its multi-level government from ALGOL,
        and having very rigid social institutions.  PASCAL was based on a
        constitution which the citizenry found necessary to break, and new
        constitutions were proposed by different parties (such as: MODULA,
        PORTAL, EDISON, etc.).
C       A monarchy with social institutions similar to PASCAL, but having
        considerably less red tape, and lacking a constitution.  C helped
        to set up the international trade organization named UNIX.
FORTH   A monarchy which, due to its extremely simple system of administration,
        won considerable support in the developing nations.
MESA    A conglomerate of states which developed reliable communication among
        the states, and made the government more accountable to the citizens.
PORTAL  One of several parties vying for the rulership of PASCAL, placing even
        greater emphasis on law and order.
SIMULA  A monarchy, much like ALGOL, in which personal ownership and private
        enterprise were allowed.
SMALLTALK
        An experiment in self government which, like SIMULA, allowed free
        enterprise; and which extended personal rights to allow groups to
        freely assemble and take on collective projects, not accountable to
        any central authority.  In this way, vast amounts of government
        duplicity were eliminated.
ADA     A very complex corporate state having many loopholes for the wealthy,
        a massive military establishment, and imperialistic ambitions.

                       NULL, BUT NOT VOID

||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||

From: REX::LEICHTERJ
To:   ALIEN::PARMENTER ! SENT TO ALIEN::PARMENTER
Subject: It's just this big hole in space, see...

Steve Gutfreund (once of the RSTS group and the Corporate Research Group, now,
like me, off in the academic world for a while) got to talking about some
generalizations we'd like to see.  In particular, consider the null device.
Why not:

        The null node.  Sending a file, say, to NULL::<anything> always
        succeeds.  Receiving from it gives an immediate end of file.

        Going in the other direction, we could have the null directory -
        appears on every device, contains all files - but they are all
        bit-buckets when written to, empty when read; the null file -
        appears in every directory, with the obvious properties; etc.

        Moving away from files, we have things like the null user - you can
        always send him mail, but you never get any from him.  Logging
        in to his account works but, of course, you get the null directory
        on the null device as your default and the null CLI - which, as
        expected, accepts any input and never produces any output.

        An interesting concept is the null message - useful when you have
        to do a PUTMSG call but have nothing to say.  There are actually
        a number of null messages - one for each non-null severity.  Thus,
        you could get:

                %-F-FATNULL-

        for the fatal null message.  And then there's the null severity...

As you can see, the concept of nullity has not been adequately explored.
I haven't even touched on applications in programming languages (null
variables, procedures, etc.), null OS services...

From: DELPHI::DELIA        "Clark D'Elia"
To:   KERMIT::PARMENTER
Subject: I'm flattered that you remembered

I double-checked with my fellow tape-mangler, Paul Kobar, and we both
agreed that in fact it was just a 600-foot magtape we unwound. The story
goes something like this ...

In software project leader land, you have to be aware of a number of
trivialities involved in getting products into and out of the SDC. One
of these concerns the proper way to construct magtape kits. (I learned
of this one while still working for Howard Lev on the RSX-11M V3.0
release; he obviously did not know about it and was on vacation at
the time.) There is an SDC guideline stating that any magtape should
not be more than 90% full. (Just for grins, quiz your project leader
to see if he/she is aware of that fact.) Here's why ...

        - It is likely that the size of the inter-record gap (the empty
          space between records on the tape) will vary slightly from
          tape drive to tape drive. It is also possible that if your
          drive writes them at the minimum end and their drive writes
          them at the maximum end and there are lots of records on
          the tape, this difference can be significant.

        - It is also likely that the stated and actual physical lengths
          of magtapes are not exactly the same. Again, if you created your
          kit on a tape that was somewhat longer than stated, and someone
          tries to copy it to a tape that is somewhat shorter than stated,
          you end up running into the little reflector strip at the end of
          the tape.

        - The SDC tape-copying programs will complain loudly if they encounter
          the reflector strip while writing the output tape.

A combination of the above happened to us during the submission of RSX-11M
V3.0 and I was one of the people that had to figure out why the SDC could
not copy some of our master tapes. So, after that we always created a set
of "short" master tapes to prepare SDC kits.

After submitting RSX-11M V3.2 (really was V4.0 in disguise), the SDC again
complained about one of our tape kits, giving us a very non-specific reason
but hinting that the tape volume was too full. So just to prove to ourselves
and to them that in fact the tape kit was an appropriate length, Paul and I
unrolled one and measured it.

I can't remember what the eventual diagnosis of the problem was, but it clearly
was not a problem with an over-full tape. By the way, the box of unrolled tape
sat in my office for a while after that -- no one volunteered to rewind it.

- Clark
