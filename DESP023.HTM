Here in RSXland, we think real-time programming is the only stuff
there is and everything else might as well be written in interpreted
BASIC.  I assume most of you saw the original, much-forwarded version
of this.  I took out a lot of stuff I either didn't understand or
agree with, or that didn't apply here and added a few more.  At any
rate, here, heavily edited, is some stuff about how to tell real
programmers.

Old joke:  You can always tell a real programmer, but you can't tell
them much.

Who says real programmers don't eat from vending machines?
||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||||

Real programmers never finish their programs, but they can always
finish them in a couple of days from "now".  Real programmers put
dirty words in their labels.  Real programmers work for computer
companies.  Real programmers don't write applications.  Real
programmers don't write microcode either. Real programmers don't use
flow charts.  Real programmers don't write specs.  Real programmers
don't comment their code.  Real programmers write readable code, which
they refuse to explain. Real programmers don't play video games; they
write them. Real programmers reboot the system and analyze the crash
dump whenever it goes down. Real programmers have read the standards
manual, but won't admit it. Real programmers don't dress for success
unless they are trying to convince others that they are going on
interviews. Real programmers don't know what systems analysts do, but
they know they could do it better.  Real programmers drink too much
coffee so that they will always seem tense   and overworked. Real
programmers always have a better idea. Real programmers can do
anything in half a day or eight lines of code, whichever is quicker.
Real programmers can do octal, hexadecimal and binary math in their
heads. Real programmers don't write memos.  Real programmers don't
know anything about business. Real programmers can't spell quiche.
Real programmers write their own sorts. Real programmers think an
eight-hour day is for sissies. Real programmers loathe documentation.
Real programmers wouldn't use the word "loathe".  Real programmers are
single.  Real programmers don't do maintenance. Real programmers eat
any damn thing, including cold pizza.  Real programmers roll up their
shirt sleeves. Real programmers don't drink decaffeinated coffee. Real
programmers never refuse a drink. Real programmers smoke. Real
programmers read science fiction, only science fiction.  Real
programmers drive Volkswagen Beetles. Real programmers make lousy
managers. Real programmers started as operators, in high school.  Real
programmers don't work for banks or insurance companies more than a
year. Real programmers don't work for city, state or federal
governments at all. Real programmers code in their heads.  Real
programmers debug in their heads, but they like piles of listings

        I happened to see in some of your mail the other day
a discussion of the putative origins of the phrase "bells and whistles".
I have a small contribution.

        In England, there had been a long tradition of street vendors
using noisemakers to attract attention to their wares.  At one point,
parliament passed regulations to reduce noise so that "untuned bells"
were outlawed. presumably that allowed street musicians to continue
to play.  At any rate, the Victorians expressed part of their disdain
for trade by the phrase "bells and whistles", something that has little
to do with the real business but that catches the attention of the
undiscerning and vulgar crowd.

        There was also a bit of a sneer at those who had to resort to
"bells and whistles" in contrast to the more dignified members of
society, whose importance was made evident by other means.

        I think that fits in with the current use fairly well.  The last
tie-in with computers is that C. Babbage was violently opposed to any
noise made by street vendors, but especially to organ grinders.  I doubt
he actually used the phrase, but he wrote numerous letters to the local
police to enforce the law, and petitioned the authorities to ban
hawking and such on his street.  Cranky guy, judging from his letters.

        / Milt

From: REX::MINOW
To:   ALIEN::PARMENTER
Subject: DCL -- useful feature, not a hack this time

Since Jerry Leichter broached the subject of "interesting" DCL
capabilities, here is a very nice feature that was added in VMS
3.1.  It gives you about 70% of the capabilities of MMS or
Unix Make in a few lines of DCL.  I've added it to a number of
command procedures that (for example) create an object file from
a source file.  This is, of course, my "model" file and may contain
typos.  Feel free to pass it on.

$! MINIMAKE.COM         build output if necessary.  Martin Minow
$       was_verify = 'f$verify(0)'
$!
$! The SOURCE file is processed to create the OUTPUT file.  This command
$! checks creation dates and does not compile if both files are present
$! and the OUTPUT file is newer (younger) than the SOURCE file.
$! This sequence defines the SOURCE and OUTPUT files:
$       SOURCE = P1
$       if SOURCE .eqs. "" then inquire SOURCE "Source File"
$       t = f$parse(SOURCE,,, "TYPE")
$!
$! Bug: f$parse can't tell the difference between "FOO" and "FOO."
$!
$       if t .eqs. "." .or. t .eqs. "" then SOURCE = "''SOURCE'.C"
$       fname = f$parse(SOURCE,,, "NAME")
$       OUTPUT = "''fname'.OBJ"
$!
$! Continue at must_process if either file is missing or the source is younger.
$! A missing SOURCE is, of course, an error -- but one that should be
$! caught by the "normal" command.
$!
$       on error then goto must_process
$       if f$search(SOURCE) .eqs. "" then goto must_process
$       if f$search(OUTPUT) .eqs. "" then goto must_process
$       src_time = f$file_attributes(SOURCE, "CDT")     ! Get creation time
$       out_time = f$file_attributes(OUTPUT, "CDT")     ! for both files.
$       if f$cvtime(src_time) .ges. f$cvtime(out_time) then -
            goto must_process
$       write sys$output OUTPUT, " is up to date."
$       goto finish
$!
$! Come here to build OUTPUT from SOURCE
$!
$ must_process:
$       on error then goto finish
$!
$! Insert commands to create OUTPUT from SOURCE here, for example:
$!
$       write sys$output OUTPUT, " <= ", SOURCE
$       cc/nolist 'SOURCE'
$!
$ finish:
$       if was_verify then set verify

As far as Indian things, the first show of disrespect to "native" Americans
(they didn't come from here originally either, you know) was calling them
Indians, which of course they are not.  Although I don't care to get into
long discussions about who taught who about guerrilla warfare, when seen
from the eyes of the English-speaking residents of New England the Indians
(of New India?) were considered somewhat treacherous.  They would often
ambush unsuspecting colonists walking through what seemed to be harmless
sections of woodland or meadow.  Thus the English-speakers put a new
phrase in their language, "Indian Summer", a warm spell that puts you off
your guard for the snow and ice lying in ambush.

The various Indian languages have phrases that reflect on the deplorable
behaviour of the white man too, so we probably don't have to appologise for
"Indian Summer" or "Indian Giver", although we should appologise for "Indian
Pudding" since it neither comes from India nor has Indians in it.  But that's
one for the FDA to settle.  On the whole, since I have never killed an Indian,
nor had one attack me, I consider the whole question of who did/is/was what
to be moot.
