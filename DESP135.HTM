Subj:   More G+S from the 14th century

Imagine the second act of "Ruddigore".  Robin has just discovered that he
is the one with the curse, and sings his song about how he is going to be
a bad fellow from now on.  Now, consider the following text.

        Storming with indignation, afflicted past relief,
        My sorrow lacks remission, I question all belief.
        This my mortal element will one day come to grief,
        Plaything to the blustering winds, as brittle as a leaf.

        When choosing a location, the wise man will select
        A bedrock as foundation, his interests to protect,
        But I, the fool, am washed along, in my own refuse wrecked,
        A firm and solid footing, I everywhere neglect.

        To what then do I compare?  To a skipperless craft,
        To a bird tumbling through the air, blown away by the draft.
        No chains are there to bind me, no keeper holds the key.
        I seek such as remind me of my depravity.

        I consider the troubled thought to sport mal apropos,
        Sweet dissolution is my lot; what better way to go?
        What Venus bids her favorite rake (let dullards sleep the day)
        I'll not disdain to undertake, a consummate roue.

        The road abounds with ample vice; each will I sample twice.
        Virtue's a chore I've no need for, I'll pay damnation's price.
        To self-indulgence do I turn, salvation's promise spurn -
        Roast my soul, take Satan his toll, the flesh is my only concern.

Pretty Gilbertian, but actually this is a translation, by Jeffrey Duban,
assistant professor of Classics at Georgia State University, of the
section "In the Tavern" from "Carmina Burana" by Carl Orff.  The original
text, in Latin, was written by monks in the monastery of Benediktbeuern
in southern Germany during the 14th century.   The original starts
out:

        Estuans interius ira vehementi
        in amaritudine loquor mee menti:
        factus de materia levis elementi
        similis sum folio de quo ludunt venti.

I think Professor Duban has spent too much time in music halls.

Three cheers! _Carmina Burana_ was my introduction to choral music, and
that's analogous to meeting somebody who is to become supremely important to
you by having him walk up to you, say "Buss me, my lass, ha-ha!",
plant a resounding smacker on your innocent cheek, then swat you on
the bottom. Rowdy, it is, and instantly addictive. There's an excellent
recording by Rafael Fruhbeck de Burgos, with Lucia Popp and her lovely
voice singing the soprano solo bits (roughly akin in difficulty
to Zerbinetta's aria from _Ariadne auf Naxos_), now available at discount
prices from Angel records. It's schlock, God knows, but when the brain
says "Nuts" and the blood says "Goodie!", who are we to cavil?

I live in Worcester, MA and I never lost power.  I believe that this can
be primarily attributed to the fact that the city requires that the
utilities be underground.  In older neighborhoods you will often find
poles running through backyards.  There are a few outlying sections of the
city that do have wires crossing the streets, when you enter one of these
areas you notice that something is different, but it takes you a while to
realize what it is.  One of the reasons that you no longer see trolley cars
in Worcester is that it was hard to prevent electrocution when you had to
put the wires on the ground.

From:   FURILO::KAISER       "Pete Kaiser, 225-5441, HLO2-1/N10"

Subj:   What I Saw on My

Is everyone back now, and is it all right to write "What I Saw on My Summer
Vacation"?  Actually "What I Saw on The Way Back From My Summer Vacation".

My new sister-in-law is a flight attendant on Air Inter, the airline on which I
returned from Nice to Paris.  We happened to be on the same flight (she was
deadheading home), and she arranged for me to sit in the cockpit on the landing
at Orly.  Now I understand why the passengers' windows are on the sides of the
plane (no architectural arguments, please): if everyone looked out the front,
they'd never fly again.  From about 10 km out, the landing strip looks about the
size of your thumbnail, AND IT NEVER GETS ANY BIGGER!  You just go lower and
lower and lower until somehow instead of flying, you're driving on an abnormally
wide road at a strangely high speed.  It was thrilling.  It was also pretty keen
to see how the cockpit crew interacts with hardly a word, reaching over each
other in every direction, it seemed, to pull, turn, or switch several thousand
apparently unmarked controls.

I'm sure this is humdrum to anyone who actually knows how to fly a plane -- it
was to the flight engineer [? -- anyhow, he wasn't in one of the pilot seats],
because when we had come to a stop at the terminal, and I said to him "I'm
beginning to believe that Man has been on the moon," he responded seriously "You
didn't believe it?"

The next exciting thing was getting a seat on my charter flight from Orly to
Boston at the worst-arranged check-in desk in the world.  Orly has a tiny little
area for charter check-in, and there were at least 4 flights accepting people at
once.  The entire room was filled shoulder-to-shoulder, and I was travelling
alone (my wife and daughter stayed in Nice with Luce's mother for another couple
of weeks), so I didn't dare leave for food or pit stop.  And I was lugging about
80 lb of luggage, household things that had to be schlepped back to the States.
When I got to the front of the line I made a wonderful discovery: my ability to
be articulately agitated in French, which happened when I asked for a nonsmoking
seat and the lady behind the desk told me there were none left.  Whatever I said
to her (and I certainly don't remember it in detail, but I do remember waving my
hands a lot), she left her position and hunted down some nonsmoking-area board-
ing passes.  I got 60A, which turned out to be the front left window seat in the
bubble of the 747.  Now, this was a chartered-configured 747, which means that
the bubble wasn't a haven of first class privilege, just the same narrow seats,
two by two; but we did have several advantages: no movie, our own galley, no
waiting for toilets, and plenty of room for carry-on luggage.  Only one disad-
vantage: the inescapable audibility of the young lady in the right rear seat
relating in boring and vapid detail to her seatmate "what Spain is like" (she
had been there a couple of days), "what Italy is like" (likewise), "what France
is like" ....  At least now I feel in touch with Today's Youth.

Not long before we landed in Boston I looked across my row.  We were all reading
books.  The title of the one I was reading is longer than the entire contents of
an average television series, and you wouldn't believe me anyhow, so I won't put
it here.  The other three persons' books were titled

        "Fame & Fortune"
        "The Wheel of Fortune"
        "Fortune"

Do you think this means something?  Should we all be going for the MBAs after
all?
