From:   GALAXY::ROLL::USENET       "USENET Newsgroup Distributor"
To:     GALAXY::PARSONS
Subj:   USENET net.usenet newsgroup articles

        When contributing to any of the USENET newsgroups, please
        be sure to give them a return mail address somewhere in
        the message ( especially if you are asking for responses
        via mail ). The USENET community is not really aware of
        the gateway and how to use it, so you have to tell them
        how to get mail to you.

        The USENET mail is similar to old Phase II DECnet, in that
        you have to specify the entire path the mail message will
        take. An adopted practice in the USENET newsgroups is to
        give a return address from a "well known" node ( or several ).
        Most people would know what the path was to the well known node,
        and thus would be able to send mail to you. Below are some example
        return addresses for ROLL::FEATHERSTON ( UUCP is the protocol used
        on the USENET, ARPA is the return address if someone wishes to send
        you mail from the ARPANET.

        Examples:

        UUCP:           ... decvax!decwrl!rhea!roll!featherston
                        ...allegra!decwrl!rhea!roll!featherston
                        ... ucbvax!decwrl!rhea!roll!featherston

        ARPA:           decwrl!rhea!roll!featherston@Berkeley
                        decwrl!rhea!roll!featherston@SU-Shasta

        It is very important to get "rhea" in the string. We have a lot
        of duplicate names between the USENET and the ENET. If they don't
        send it through decwrl<->rhea, it could end up on a totally
        foreign system.

                                        /ed featherston/

From: ORPHAN::LIONEL       "Steve Lionel"
To:   DESPERADO
Subject: Big Apple meets Uppity Ursula

Many DEC product announcements make the newspapers, and most even make the
computer magazines, but it is rare to see a non-technical publication pick up
on a DEC product introduction, especially so when they don't just crib from
the press release.

Therefore, it was with total astonishment that I read the following item in
the "Talk of the Town" section of the January 2 New Yorker:

Voices from Nowhere

Out there on the frontier, smack up against the great or ghastly
unknown, it can get, oh, so very quiet.  Sometimes the only sound is
your heartbeat. (Sometimes there are chain saws.)  But stay out there
long enough and it's a sure bet that you'll begin to hear voices.
Voices that belong to no man. Voices from Nowhere.  These last weeks,
we've spent a little time with the pioneers on America's newest
frontier- the uncharted wilderness of high tech. And we've heard some
voices.

To operate DECtalk, a new way for computers to talk with an unlimited
vocabulary, you type the words you wish to hear on a keyboard -
"Fourscore and seven years ago," perhaps - and then hit the return key.
 Instantly, in the unmistakeable tones of an inebriated Swede speaking
English, DECtalk delivers your message.  Larry Twaits, the DECtalk
product manager at Digital Equipment Corporation, was standing on the
sidelines at the press conference where DECtalk announced its entry
into the market.  How, we asked him, did the four-thousand-dollar box
of logic circuits - chips, bits, bytes - learn to talk?

"It was like magic," he said, and for a second we understood.  But
then Twaits launched into a long and devious spiel about "computer
models of the human vocal track" and the calculation of sixteen
variables, including the size of sinus cavities, and by the time he
was done we were reduced to asking, "Can it sing?"

Yes, this one can, provided you request "Strangers in the Night".

A roomful of adult reporters listened respectfully as DECtalk showed
off at its press conference.  It spoke in five of its eight voices (an
extended family of soused Scandanavians, from a grade-schooler named
Kit the Kid right up through Frail Frank, a senior citizen), and
recited "Peter Piper picked" at three hundred and fifty words per
minute, and introduced a lot of Digital Equipment executives, none of
whom performed tongue twisters or anything else.  Instead, they talked
about bisynchronous terminal support, and the automated office of the
future, and the awsome number of potential applications for DECtalk,
which is being used at the Children's Hospital in Boston to speak to
young children who are non-speaking or have very severe speech
difficulties.

When the press conference ended, we sidled up to a keyboard and put
the machine through its paces.  "War is peace," it burbled dutifully.
For a moment, we couldn't think of anything else we wanted it to say,
but only for a moment.  We carefully selected Voice Four, Uppity
Ursula, typed in our message, and then leaned back to hear her say, "I
find you fascinating and handsome- indeed, the fairest of them all."
