On another geographical front, New York, New York, the city so nice they
named it twice, has screwed up so many things for so long that I and most
other native New Yorkers I know are pleasantly suprised to find that they
have done something right.

In the Riverdale section of the Bronx, just west of the Saw Mill River
Parkway, stands a twenty-story building with metal shutters on the
outside of its windows and a copse of antennae on its roof. This
building, between 254th and 255th streets, serves as living quarters
for the Soviet mission to the UN.  Around it is a 12-foot tall fence
made of what look like wrought-iron javelins, and in front of it is a
small guard house, manned by one of New York's finest.

Across from this building is a small park, just a few thousand square
feet of coblestones, a few trees and shrubs, and a couple of park
benches.  The park was once named for some minor hero of local
significance, but the City has renamed it Shcharansky Square.

Anatoly Shcharansky is an enemy of the Union of Soviet Socialist
Republics, an enemy who has indulged in counterrevolutionary activity
by spreading illegal and untrue Western propaganda regarding so-called
violations of the Helsinki Accords by the Motherland, and this despite
the fact that it is well-known that the Helsinki Accords are little
more than a thinly disguised plot by the West to besmirch the Soviet
People and their fight for class equality.  For this, Shcharansky has
been shipped 5000 miles east of Moscow, stripped of his job, his
books, his access to professional literature - He is a physicist -
denied communication with his family, denied an exit visa, jailed,
humiliated, and abused.

I thought it a nice touch by His Honor, the Mayor, to name the front
yard of the Soviet appartment building Shcharansky Square because it
forces the Soviet citizens who live there to confront the lie that
they live every day.

Could this be the dreaded 'Real Programmer Syndrome'?
Article from Sunday Times, 13 March by Peter Wilby:

A new type of drug addict is worrying university teachers: the
computer junkie, who skips lectures for a daily fix at the terminal.
Students have been known to clock up 35 hours in four days on the
computer including 17 without a break.

Now the dons are monitoring students' use of computers and, at Brunel
University, addicts are rationed to five hours a week. Those who take
an overdose, may lose marks on course work.

Most addicts are studying computer science, but physics and
engineering students may also fall victim. Chris Reynolds, reader in
computer science at Brunel, says junkies almost invariably do badly in
exams because they concentrate on programming to the exclusion of
other areas.

He fears some students may spend five hours a night on home computers
in their halls of residence.

Computers are addictive, he says, because they are neat, tidy and
logical. "People talk to computers in the same way as children talk to
dolls. There are not likely to be any social conflicts."

Reynolds says students are encouraged by schools and parents to apply
for computer science because they have spent so much time on home
computers. "In fact, the technology they pick up isn't much use to
them because, in terms of computer languages, it's archaic. And their
enthusiasm may be the result of some personality problem. I fear that
computer science is tending to attract introverts"

From: ALIEN::SPIDER::COLLIER
To:   ALIEN::PARMENTER
Subject: Cliche resolved???

I have been fruitlessly worrying for some time about the meaning/origin of
the phrase 'the whole nine yards.'  Whenever it was used in my presence
I asked the speaker what it meant; responses have always been the
equivalent of ' . . . duhhhhhhhh . . .'  But we may have a solution!!!
After several months of well deserved harassment to establish a meaning
or stop babbling the phrase, Ron Uberti has come through.  He suggests
it might mean 'the whole nine cubic yards of concrete' in a cement-truck
load.  E.g., 'how much of this load do you want dumped for the rear patio?'
'I want the whole nine yards.'  Ron said this explanation 'sounds intuitively
right to me,' and I agree.  A quick check on the blackboard with dimly
remembered solid geometry kept hopes alive, and an equally quick check with
a local contractor confirmed that while the smallest cement truck size
is eight cubic yards, and there are many different larger sizes, that
nine cubic yards is vary definitely one of the standard sizes.  I propose
that Desperadi vote unanimously that this be accepted as the final solution.

        A TOUR OF THE SIMPSON SPRING BOTTLING PLANT IN EASTON, MASS.

        Here's a tour that may appeal to those intrigued by old
machinery and one that provides handson experience in the way things
were in the 1920's.

The Simpson Spring Company of South Easton is a survivor amoung a
small group of independent soft-drink manufacturers in the US today,
and on the first Saturday of each month, though October, the plant
is open to the public.  There is a small museum where anyone with
a hankering for the mechanical can operate bottle cappers and other
machinery of bygone days.  A guided tour of the plant itself, in
full operation, leads you through the syrup room, where the oils
of vanilla, sweet birch, and cofee are made into bases for cream,
white birch, and coffee sodas; then continues to the laboratory
and the white-tiled spring room, where you can glimpse the source
of the sparkling water used in their sodas; and ends with as visit
to the bottling line.

The Simpson Spring Factory building has remained virtually unchanged
since it was designed in the '20's.  Company president Edwin White is
proud of his product, especially the all-time favorite - cofee soda.
Simpson Spring also produces Moxie and, as an added attraction, the
Moxie Horsemobile, a 1930 LaSalle with the colorful Moxie Mascot
mounted on its hood, is parked out front.  According to White, it's
"there for kids to clamber on."

Tours of the Simpson Springs plant are scheduled for September 6
and October 4, between 9 a.m and 2 p.m.  Any group larger than
15 should make arrangments by calling 238-4472.  The plant is located
just off Route 138, in South Easton.
