from  BOSTON SUNDAY GLOBE   for  6 April 1986:

'FIRST' COMPUTER LINK FOUND BY MUSEUM?
by Rob Stein, UPI

A contest aimed at locating and saving artifacts that help trace the
development of the personal computer has unearthed a primitive machine
that might rewrite the history of the popular device.

Among 316 entries for the contest sponsored by The Computer Museum of
Boston was a machine known as the Kenbak 1 that could be the first
personal computer, according to Oliver Strimpel, curator of the museum.

"It's exciting to see these early examples," Strimpel said Friday.

The museum solicited entries for the contest over the past year and
received offerings from across the United States and 13 other nations
including France and Australia.

The contest was aimed at gathering important artifacts that could be
used to trace the development of personal computers, which have become
so popular over the last few years, he said.

The entries included a wide range of early software, manuals, advertising
and equipment, and several machines predating what had previously been
believed to be the first personal computer, Strimpel said.

Most computer historians had thought the Altair 8800, made by MTS Inc.
in Albuquerque, N.M., in 1975, was the first commercially produced
personal computer.

But the museum has received at least two entries manufactured and
marketed before the Altair, including one produced in 1971, Strimpel
said.

The Kenbak 1, made by the Kenbak Corp. of Los Angeles, sold for
about $750 and was apparently aimed at the educational market,
Strimpel said.

The 14-pound blue and silver machine, a metal box with a series of
buttons and lights on the front, could be programmed to do simple
functions such as arithmetic, he said.  The machine had a mere 256
bytes of memory, compared to the 1 million bytes that many personal
computers in use today offer.

Although the machine was far from a commercial success -- only about
40 were sold -- it appears to be the earliest version of what evolved
into today's Apple MacIntosh and IBM PC, Strimpel said.

The criteria for judging will be the entry's significance to the
development of personal computers, its rarity, originality and
condition, he said.

"So many people from California to France were putting microprocessors
into boxes in the 1970s and we've only heard about a few that made it
into the light of day through commercial success," said Strimpel.
