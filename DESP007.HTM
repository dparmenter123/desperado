From: HYDRA::MIDDLETON
To:   ALIEN::PARMENTER
Subject: Hyper everything.

DEC Manuals are perhaps the worst I've ever seen.  By comparison, the manuals
we used in the Navy were terrific.  (In general I didn't like the Navy much,
and at the time I thought the manuals were terrible.  Little did I know that
industry manuals were even worse.)  Anyhow, over the 10 years I've been at DEC
I've learned to put up with almost anything, even PDP-11/40 Hardware and VMS
Software manuals.  Nevertheless, in the UDA50 Programmers Doc. Kit manuals I
think we have hit a new low.  For example, the following quote is from page
4-27 of the MSCP Basic Disk Functions Manual which is part of the above
mentioned Doc. Kit:

        As stated above, the host area of a disk is structured as a
        vector of logical blocks.  From a performance viewpoint, however,
        it is more appropriate to view the host area as a four
        dimensional hyper-cube, the four dimensions being cylinder,
        group, track, and sector.
                        .
                        .
                        .
        Referring to our hyper-cube analogy, the set of potentially accessible
        blocks form a line parallel to the track axis.  This line moves
        parallel to the sector axis, wrapping around when it reaches the edge
        of the hyper-cube.

Now, are we supposed to visualize a four-dimensional hyper-cube (a good trick)
or the three-dimensional representation (see "And He Built A Crooked House" by
Robert Heinlein)?  Maybe we are supposed to use the three-dimensional
representation to map a Grey Code sequence.  Who knows?  Who cares?!

Another fun example comes from page 2-2 of the same manual, the Terminology
chapter:

        Nugatory

            Of little or no consequence:  Trifling, Inconsequential.  See
            Section "AVAILABLE Command"

Using the Table of Contents we wend our way around to the "AVAILABLE Command"
on page 6-9 and find:

        It is nugatory to issue this command to a unit that is "Unit-Available"
        unless the "Spin-down" modifier is specified.

I know nugatory is a real word, but I still think it would be more effective to
say "It will have no effect" or "It will do nothing" or "Nothing will happen".

Anyhow, I thought you might enjoy these examples of Great Technical Writing.  I
hope this isn't the shape of things to come.

                                                J.M.

From: EVE::RUZICH

Subject: Elementary Basic

"Elementary Basic" by Ledgard and Singer is actually the best introduction to
Basic for non-technical people I've ever seen.  I bought it for my wife.

There are, of course, dialogues between Holmes and Watson in Conan Doyle's
style. The Basic programming enters into it when they are solving mysteries.
For example, they want to figure out whether it is possible for a particular
suspect to travel by train from Birmingham to London with a long enough
stopover at Oxford to commit the crime.  Naturally, Homles says this is "an
excellent case for the analytical engine, Watson", and they proceed to program
it, ostensibly on Babbage's machine.  Babbage is a character, by the way.

Another mystery is titled "The Ciphered Message", which introduces ASCII.

They even manage to get into programming style in a discussion of top-down
analysis.

Most Basic books start out with quadratic equations in the first chapter.
"Elementary Basic", on the other hand, assumes that the reader is intelligent,
but does not know algebra.
