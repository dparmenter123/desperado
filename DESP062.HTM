From: CLOSUS::OSTROWSKY
To:   TOMP
Subject: You'll scoop the world with this one

I was fortunate enough to attend an exhibitors' preview of "Superman III"
this past weekend.  If you're a fan of these films (and be honest with
yourself -- when the temperature and humidity both soar over 90 this July,
are you really going to quibble about plot and characterization?  Don't be
silly), you'll definitely like this third installment, which was directed
entirely by Richard Lester, and which stars, in addition to Christopher
Reeve, Annette O'Toole as Lana Lang, Robert Vaughan as the evil (pronounced
ee-vilh) Ross Webscoe, and Richard Pryor as Gus Gorman.  Margot Kidder, who
was almost written out of this one, makes only cameo appearances.

Lester's work includes "A Hard Day's Night," "The Three Musketeers," "The
Knack (and How to Get It)", "The Ritz," "A Funny Thing Happened on the Way
to the Forum," and part of "Superman II."  He's an outstanding film-maker,
in my opinion, and does a pretty good job with this movie.  The first half
or so is fast and often funny, which is to say vintage Lester.  The second
half gets somewhat bogged down, unfortunately, due to the grim way Superman's
latest personal crisis is portrayed.  The movie becomes somewhat schizophrenic
at that point, which is a shame, because large parts of it are marvelous.

It wouldn't be entirely accurate to say it's a "Richard Pryor film," but
there's no doubt the movie revolves around his character.  Pryor is quite
good, although he doesn't break any new ground.  Reeve and O'Toole make a
nice couple.  And, of special interest to DESPERAOs, much of the plot
revolves around computers.

All in all, I give it 3 capes.  If you see it with an emptier bladder than
I did, and are further unencumbered by the presence of two loud and stupid
people immediately behind you, you may enjoy it even more than I did.

--J.

PS -

And a special tip o' Clio's hat to British Airways and their ad agency for
their new ad campaign.  The first commercial, in which they show graphically
how many people they fly per year, is an outstanding combination of parts of
"Close Encounters of the Third Kind" and James Blish's saga beyond the stars,
"Cities in Flight."  Even looks good in 12" B&W.

From: RSXHUB::ZEKE::ORC::WALSH
To:   KERMIT::T_PARMENTER

     This past Sunday night, May 15th, I was able to attend the PHC Roadshow
     performance that has made its way to the east coast.  I was able to get
     tickets thanks to the telephone system, plastic money, and the slow
     response of New Yorkers to snap up the tickets to this fine show.
     We ended up with seats in NY's Town Hall (not City Hall, and I don't
     know the history behind the name) that were in the upper balcony,
     (a.k.a. the stratosphere) about 4 rows from the back.  The view of the
     stage was fine, since the slope to the upper balcony approached that of
     an A-frame roof!!  The show was started at 8:00 PM SHARP!! (Radio
     discipline, I guess) and featured the following regulars from PHC:

                Garrison Keillor (of course)
                The Butch Thompson Trio (himself, Bill Evans and Red Maddock)
                Stoney Lonesome (a seven or so piece bluegrass group)
                   I can't keep track of 7 people's names in one band!!
                Peter Auschrusko (string player quite good!!)
                  (Splelling??)
                And Greg Brown, folksong writer (Slow Food, The Iowa Waltz)

    The first half of the performance alone was nonstop, with the action
    shifting smoothly from one player to the next for better than an hour; and
    to see how much effort these people put into the performance was quite
    amazing.

    The show, though not broadcast, was taped, for possible broadcast at a
    later date, and the demeanor was one of a live radio braodcast. I was
    quite pleased to see Garrison himself, in his cream-colored suit, red tie,
    and white shirt.  He really does walk and talk, and does not merely stare
    out from the backs of album covers.  I found it somewhat surprising that
    for certain portions of the show Garrison just stands onstage, hands in his
    pockets, and sort of taps his feet to the music.  Many high points to the
    show...Red Maddock doing the "Clouds of Joy" Bubblebath commercial, the
    Stoney Lonesome singers doing "Answer your phone", a promo for the phone
    company, to the tune of "Praise the Lord, I saw the light", but
    probably the best was "News from Lake Woebegone", where Garrison, standing
    alone onstage spun a yarn for better than 30! minutes, with the whole
    audience hushed.   The basic theme was "Reliability, why you don't want it,
    especially when its something you don't want to come around (like exams,
    chorale practice, etc.)".  The tale ranged into hopes for signs of the
    first warm day of sping in Minnesota, wanting to sing in a church with a
    choir, not wanting to sing all by yourself in chorale practice, at least
    not in front of EVERYBODY.  Problems with being 13 years old, and being
    told to sing about, "Septmember is in my mistress' bosom," and not being
    too knowledge able about either, and quite curious to maybe sort of know
    a little more about both (i.e. mistess' and/or bosoms).  The most striking
    moment was when Garrison started to sing the refrain from the schoolkids'
    modified "battle hymn of the republic",

                Glory, Glory, How peculiar,
                Teacher hit me with a ruler,
                So I bopped her on the bean
                With a rotten tangerine,
                And she ain't gonna teach no more..

   Not so much that he sang it, but the whole audience joined in, and they
   knew it to the same lyrics !!  (is this one a schoolkids classic?)

   Other nice thoughts from the performance:

        The audience was VERY well-behaved (perhaps the shy people were an
                influence).
        With the program, they handed out a copy of Minnesota Public Radio's
               WIRELESS catalog, wich lists all the PHC goodies you can buy;
               at FAR less than the $$90.00 WGBH asks for an album..
        Also, an advace order form for the paperback version of "Happy to be
        Here", Garrison's book, as well as the 1983 PHC Tour album, when
        released.

        The greatest irony was the location of the theater; Town Hall is
        located on 43rd street near Sixth Avenue, one block north and east
        of Times Square.  An unusual juxtaposition: Sleaze, gore and basic
        obscenity a block away, and we had a packed house listening to some
        good bluegrass and folk music, dry humor, and story telling.  As Pete
        Seeger announced at GEAR-UP '81, the eastern bicyclists rally in New
        Paltz, NY; "A thousand peole gather here in the country to go out and
        ride bicycles.  Maybe there's hope for this world yet."

                                        Heaven's, they're tasty!

                                                faithful reviewer,
                                                        John Walsh

          interfering with each other.  Using  four  Digital
          Equipment  Corp.   computers  wired  into the same
          memory bank, SPREAD will explore the effectiveness
          of  different  operating systems and languages.  A
