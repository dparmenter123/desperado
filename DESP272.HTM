Subject: E-Prime

Having recently learned of the existence of E-Prime I feel duty-bound
to advise you of it.  My source is an essay entitled "'To Be' in Their
Bonnets", written by Cullen Murphy and published in his book _Just
Curious_.

E-Prime is a human language.  It is essentially English, from which
all inflections of the verb "to be" have been subtracted.  Apparently,
a large faction of E-Prime advocates exists in the world of general
semantics.  Their concern about "to be" is summed up in a passage from
George Santayana.

    The little word "is" has its tragedies.  It names and identifies
    different things with the greatest innocence; and yet no two are
    ever identical, and if therein lies the charm of wedding them and
    calling them one, therein too lies the danger.  Whenever I use the
    word "is", except in sheer tautology, I deeply misuse it; and when
    I discover my error, the world seems to fall asunder.

Practitioners of E-Prime strive to eliminate "to be" from their
writing, but a few stouthearted individuals even use E-Prime in
speech.  Murphy interviewed the most accomplished of these, and
reports

    I expected to hear someone speaking in a slow and considered
    manner, as if picking his way uncertainly through a linguistic
    swamp, but he talked briskly and with what sounded like supreme
    self-confidence.  Yes, he said, he halted a lot when he first
    started speaking E-Prime, in 1978, and a lot of sentences ended
    abruptly in the middle when he could see himself running into an
    "is".  At times he lapsed into a kind of pidgin E-Prime.  The
    transition, in short, took some work.  "I had to cope with all
    that backlog of "is"-pattern English," he explained.  "And I had
    to do it in real time."  But [he] got the hang of it stylistically
    after a year or so, and he now strives in his speech . . . to
    effect other refinements.  These include ridding his vocabulary of
    instances of absolutism (for example, such words as "always" and
    "never", which imply immutability) and of many nouns made out of
    verbs (in particular those with a "-tion" ending, such as
    "visualization" and "procrastination", which freeze ongoing
    processes into static events). . . .  "I try to move toward a
    language that communicates the territory of my experience to
    myself and others as clearly and accurately as possible."

Of course, Murphy's essay is written entirely in E-Prime.  Wanting to
avoid the headache he claims it caused, I didn't even try.

 Oh, my.  I take exception to the opening remark that "E-prime is a
 human language".  These are nothing but word games, playing that
 there is something mathematical or philosophical about verbal
 expression.  Who says "is" means "equals"?  (Indeed, as we know from
 Lisp, there are degrees of equality, that is EQ means "equal to the
 same thing" while EQUAL means "the same thing".)  We all understand
 that "always" may be no more than "often" and "never" may only be
 "rarely".  The objection to nouns made of verbs is sheer snobbery.
 If these folks are sincere (I see you smirking, Mr. Santayana.),
 they'd also be eliminating all figures of speech from the language.
 Only the Laconians are laconic and the only mammals are females and a
 pinafore must be pinned on the forepart and a bonfire is only for
 burning bones.

 The verb "to be" has a set of meanings just like any other word in
 the language.  Does Santayana object to these meanings of "to be"?

        "exist" -- "To be or not to be"
        "happen" -- "the wedding is tomorrow"
        "located" -- "the hat is on his head"
        "belong or befall" -- "The force is with him"
        "remain" -- "Let it be, E Primers"

 As the Jewish granny of the story says, "Smart smart.  Dumb dumb".
